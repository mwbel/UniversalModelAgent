# Retrospect of Volume II

In Volume II, various classical methods for computing the perturbations are given together with their modern versions,with a view to their application to the motion of artificial satellites and interplanetary probes. All these methods are, unfortunately, not uniformly convergent, as will be shown in Volume III,and they are only semiconvergent in Poincaré's terminology.They may be convenient for predicting the motions within the present-day accuracy of observation. The question is whether the result would approach nearer the as yet unknown true solution if we push the approximation further in order to meet the demands in the future of increasing accuracy of observation.The circumstance may be similar to Stirling's asymptotic representation of factorials； that is,a stage might come when the approximation would break down ifwe push the approximation further and further.Even the estimation of the errors of the approximation cannot be made so far.By some of the modern methods which will be treated in the following volumes we may estimate the errors of the approximation.Then what will be the form of the solution of the planetary theory and the many-body problem -in particular, the three-body problem? This will be one of the topics of Volume III. Also,the difficult problem of stability will be dealt with according to Liapounov's work and its modern version.
