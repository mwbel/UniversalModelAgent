So far we have discussed the particular solution of the three-body problem in which the masses are all quite arbitrary.Now we consider a particular solution obtained by restricting the masses，and examine whether there is a solution where the masses form an isosceles triangle. Fransen (1895) discussed three kinds of isosceles-triangular solutions : one with an axis of symmetry, one with a plane of symmetry,and one with both an axis of symmetry and a plane of symmetry- the axis of symmetry being on the plane of symmetry. These are particular cases for equal masses in Euler's solution and in Lagrange's equilateraltriangular solution.Pavanini (l907）and MacMillan （i9l3） calculated the solution for the case with an axis of symmetry, that is,when two of the bodies move in circular orbits on a plane perpendicular to the axis of symmetry and the third body (with infinitesimal mass) moves along the axis of symmetry. Buchanan (l920) obtained periodic solutions and asymptotic solutions in their neighborhood when the two bodies with equal masses move in circular orbits and in elliptic orbits,according to Poincaré's theory of periodic solutions.Wilczynski (l9l3） applied projective differential geometry (Wilczynski, l906；Fubini and Cech, 1926) to a generalization ot Euler's and Lagrange's solutions. Supposing the center of mass of the three bodies to be at rest, he considered the properties of the trajectories of each of the three bodies. Two of these trajectories lie upon the ruled surface described by the straight line joining two of the three bodies,and all three trajectories lie upon the conical surface enveloping the instantaneous orbital planes of the three bodies (Salmon,l9l2).If the velocities of the three bodies are in the plane of the three bodies at each instant, then the orbits are all plane curves and the surface in question coincides with the common plane of the three orbital planes. Wilczynski proved that, except when at least one of the three bodies has a plane curve as its orbit, the case where one of the ruled surfaces is a developable surface or a conical surface occurs only when two sides of the triangle formed by the three bodies are equal. Furthermore, the case where the curve described by one of the three bodies is asymptotic to one of the ruled surfaces occurs only for an isosceles triangle. Seeking the condition under which the ruled surface describes a conical surface, he showed that the vertex of the conical surface recedes to infinity and the surface becomes a cylinder. Then two sides of the triangle become equal and the base of the triangle describes a cylindrical surface which is the ruled surface in question. The ruled surface may sometimes degenerate into a plane or a straight line. From this it is evident that, if two sides of the triangle are equal, then the mass at the vertex of the triangle describes a plane curve passing through the center of mass.This is the case with a plane of symmetry.In other cases where one of the masses describes a curve asymptotic to the ruled surface, the two sides of the triangle meeting at the center of mass should be equal. This is the case with an axis of symmetry. Thus Wilczynski proved the existence of particular solutions for an isosceles triangle.
