The position of the instantaneousaxisof figure of adeformableEarthissubjectto motions from distortions of the Earth,suchas the body tides.The motion ofthis point makes it difficult to interpret the motions of $F$ in terms of rigid-body concepts.It thus becomes useful to define themean surface geographic axis, $B$ ,asanaxisattached inaleast-squares sense to the Earth's outersurface.Consideranetwork of observatoriesonarigid Earthand anaxis fixedwith respect to the positions of the observatories.The only possible motion of that network is a time-dependent,rigid rotation;the portion of the rigid rotation thatis not parallel to the axis will cause motion of the axis as the latter follows motions of the observatories defining it. The conventional international origin ² was defined originally by the adopted astronomical latitudes of the five observatories contributing to the International Latitude Service (ILS) (Markowitz and Guinot1968).Itroughly coincides with the mean pole $F$ over the period from 1900 to 19O5.Later,the terrestrial pole was defined by alarger number of observatories operating optical equipment (e.g.,photographic zenith tubes)that reported to the International PolarMotion Service (IPMS) and the Bureau International de I'Heure (BIH).Both BIH and IPMS systems were compatible with the CIO.Satelite laser ranging(SLR) and VLBI(§ 6.2) became more accurate techniques for determining the position of the pole,and the BIH pole became strongly dependent on these data.In January 1988 the maintenance of the terrestrial reference frame was taken over by the International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service (IERS).The IERS maintains apole consistent within the observational errors with those of the conventional international origin and BIH.Current higher accuracy observations require a model of plate motions in the analysis of observations.This new reference frame is calledthe International TerrestrialReferenceFrame(ITRF).

# 6.2.3The Celestial Intermediate Reference Systemand theCelestial Intermediate Pole (CIP)

Prior to the 2Oo6 edition of The Astronomical Almanac the algorithms used to determine the precession and nutation of the Earth were based on the use of the Earth'srotation axis or Celestial Ephemeris Pole (CEP)rather than the CIP.The IAU(198O) defined the Celestial EphemerisPoleas having,“no nearly-diurnal nutation with respect toa space-fixed or Earthfixed coordinate system."Moritz and Mueller(1988) point out that this was often mistakenly assumed to be the axis offigure.However,the axis offigure does not conform to the definition of the CEP.Instead,the CEP is dynamically the same as the Tisserand mean polar axis.The Tisserand meanaxesare defined,as that set of axeswhere thedeformation fieldof the Earth integrated over the volume of the Earth contains no rotation (Moritz and Mueller 1988).

Beginningwith the 2oo6editionof The Astronomical Almanac thecelestial reference systemaligned with theCelestial IntermediatePole (CIP)iscalledtheCelestial Intermediate Reference System.Ideally,the CIP would be the projection of the line passing through the Earth's center of mass and the ITRF pole onto the sky.However,the free motion of the pole ofrotation within the body of the Earth is partially stochasticand,hence,cannot be predicted with certainty.

Beginningwith the 2Oo6 edition of The Astronomical Almanac the algorithmsused to determine the precession and nutation of the Earth use the CIP.The CIP is defined as the Tisserand mean axis of the Earth whose motion is comprised of those periodic components, observed inacelestial reference,that have periods greater than two days (IAU 2OOlc).For the CIP nutation is defined as the forced motions with periods greater than two days with respect to the CIP,and polar motion is defined as the free motion and the forced motions with periods lessthan or equal to two days.The forced motion is the solution of the Earth'sequations of rotational motion accounting for all external torques (specifically the gravitational forces due to the Sun,Moon,and planets).The free motionis that motion which would exist if there were no external torques on the rotating Earth (the particular solution of the differential equations).If the Earth were rigid,the free motion wobble would have a period of about 304 days.However,forthephysical, non-rigidEarth,thismotion,called theChandler wobble, has a period of about 14 months.Polar motion also includes the motions arising from changes in the distribution of the Earth's mass from the climate along with motions resulting from geophysical strains and meteorological effects.

If the two principal axes of inertia that lie in the Earth's equatorial plane were identical, then the shortest periodic motion arising from the torques of the Moon,Sun,and planets would have a period of about a month.And,the CIP would be identical to the CEP.However, these two principal axes of inertia differby about $0 . 0 0 2 \%$ (Moritz and Mueller1988).The difference in the torques about these two principal axes of inertia gives rise to small motions of the Tisserand mean axes with periods less than or approximately equal to a day.These motions have periods that are exactly equal to those of many of the tidal deformations in the Earth,and cannot be separated by observation.Thus,the requirement of dropping those terms with periods of less than two dayswas imposed on the CIP.One result of this definition is that the TRS is no longer fixed by the loosely defined Greenwich meridian but is moving eastward at about $0 . 0 1 6 \mathrm { m m } \mathrm { y r } ^ { - 1 }$ ,on average (Lambert and Bizouard 2002).

Inaddition to the free nutation observed in all spinning bodies,there is a second free motion for the Earth.This second free motion arises from the difference in the alignment of the Earth's core and mantle,and iscalled the free core nutation.It is also known as the diurnal free polar motion or nearly diurnal free wobble.It produces a periodic motion of approximately 432daysof the CIP in theGCRS.

# 6.2.3.1 The Ecliptic

The ecliptic is generally defined as the mean plane of the Earth'sorbit around the Sun. The actual plane of the Earth's orbit is constantly changing in response to the perturbations from the otherplanets in the solar system,making theactual determination of the eclipticfar from easy. One problem in realizing the ecliptic is a practical definition ofa mean plane.To reduce the uncertainties in the definition of the ecliptic the IAU Working Group on Precession and the Ecliptic (Hilton et al.2OO6) have recommended that the ecliptic be more precisely defined as the plane perpendicular to the mean orbital angular momentum vector of the Earth-Moon barycenter passing through the Sun in the BCRS.

The“inertial”and the“rotating”ecliptics.Prior to the advent of Lunar Laser Ranging (LLR)observations,it was impossible to determine whether or not the space motions of solarsystem objects contained a residual global rate of rotation.Newcomb (1898) defined the“rotating”ecliptic as the plane perpendicular to the mean motion of the Earth-Moon barycenter from the point of view of an observer co-moving with the ecliptic.For such a comoving observer,there isacomponent to the orientation of the ecliptic that arises from the motion of the ecliptic itself.Onthe other hand,Le Verrier(1855) defined the ecliptic from the point of view of an observer in inertial space.Thus,older publications used two different definitions of the ecliptic.Standish (1981) showed that the equinox based on this “rotating” definition of the mean ecliptic is 93.66 milliarcseconds (mas)to the east of the equinox based onan inertially defined ecliptic,and theobliquityof theEarth is 3.34mas greaterusing the rotating definition ofthe ecliptic than ifaninertial eclipticisused.Thus,the ecliptic definition used can cause significant errors for applications that require accuracy better than about $0 ^ { \prime \prime } 1$ The BCRS isoriented with respect to the inertial ecliptic while many,but not all,older reference systems,such as theFK5and the reference systemusedbyDE2OO (Folkner et al. 1994),are oriented with respect to the rotating ecliptic.Thus,care must be used when using older data to determine if they are referring to the inertial or rotating definition of the ecliptic.

# 6.2.4 The Equinoxes

The classic concept ofthe originof right ascension,the longitudinal coordinate of the celestial reference system,is the dynamical vernal equinox:The point onthecelestial sphere where the celestial equator intersects the ecliptic and through which the center of the mean Sun passes asitcrosses the equator going north.More specifically,it is the directionof the intersectionof the projection of the Earth's equator onto the sky with the mean orbit plane of the Earth-Moon barycenter near which the Sun passes on its way north of the Earth's equator. Inaddition to the vernal equinox,there isalso the autumnal equinox,which is the direction of the intersection between the Earth's equator and the ecliptic near where the center of the mean Sun passes going south. Generally,the use of the word “equinox”implies the vernal equinox

Likethe ecliptic,the Earth's equatoris a dynamicallydefined plane.Thus,neither plane is fixed in space,and the equinox is constantly changing.The change of the equinox with time requires thata date beaffixed to itinorder to specifya fixed direction.The reference epoch for the equinox in common use curently is J2ooo.O (Julian date $2 4 5 1 5 4 5 . 0 \mathrm { T T }$ ),see $\ S 6 . 7$

It is possible to refer to either the mean equator or the true equator.The mean equator is amoving equator prescribed only by secular (non-periodic)motion;that is,motion due to precession.The true equator,on the other hand,includes both secular and periodic motions; that is,motions due to both precession and nutation.Beginning with the 2Oo6 edition of The AstronomicalAlmanac,the equator was defined as the plane perpendicular to the CIP(§ 6.2.3) that passes through the Earth's center of mass.Prior to the 2OO6 edition the equator was defined as the plane perpendicular to the CEP( $\ S 6 . 2 . 3 )$ that passes through the Earth'scenter of mass.The existence of aseparate mean equator and true equator implies the existence of a mean equinox and a true equinox corresponding to the directions where the mean equator intersects the ecliptic or where the true equator intersects the ecliptic.The CIO( $\ S 6 . 2 . 2 )$ used inthe Celestial Intermediate Reference System is independent of the dynamical equinox.

# 6.6.3 Nutation

The non-secular motion of the Earth's rotation axis with respect to a space-fixed coordinate system iscalled nutation,and consists of both forced and free components.The forced component of the nutation arises from the torque produced by the gravitational attraction of the Moon and,to a lesser extent,the Sun and planets on the equatorial bulge and any deformations of the Earth.Itis usually modeled as a series of quasi-periodic motions whose periods and amplitudes depend on the source of the torque and the motion of the celestial bodies with respect to the rotating Earth.Seting the external force to zero in the equations ofmotion gives theparticular integral,or freemotion.This free motion depends on the constitution of the Earth including properties such as elasticity of the mantle and the size and shapesof the inner and outer cores.Itis excited by internal processes,and can be determined only from observations.If the Earth were a rigid body,it would be possible to compute thecoefficients of the forced nutation terms.However,like the freemotion,some of the parameters of the non-rigid Earth can be determined only by observation of its motion in space.

The separation of the instantaneous axis of rotation and instantaneous axis of the principal moment of inertia gives rise to nutation componentswith periods that are approximately diurnal and amplitudesaslargeasafewmilliarcseconds.Inaddition,the figure of the Earth isslightly tri-axial,meaning that there isa small difference between the magnitudesof the two principal moments of inertia that lie nearly in the Earth's equatoras defined by the axis ofrotation.This difference in the equatorial principal moments of inertia also gives rise to nearly diurnal and semidiurnal nutation components with amplitudes of no more thana few microarcseconds.Although these short-period components of the nutation are properly part of the nutation,they cannot be separated observationally from notions due to the movement of theEarth'saxiswithin the body of the Earth.Consequently,the CIP( $\ S \ 6 . 2 . 3 )$ ischosen to be along the axis offigure for the mean surface of amodel of Earth in which the motions with periods greater than or equal to two days within a geocentric celestial reference frame have zero amplitude.This pole has no nearly diurnal components of motion with respect to either a space-fixed or an Earth-fixed coordinate system,and componentsof nutation with periods shorter than two days become part of the polar motion by definition.

The CIP is the axis about which the diurnal rotation of the Earth is applied in the transformation betweencelestial and terrestrial frames,so itisoften referred toastheaxis ofrotation in general discussions.It may also be referred to as the true celestial pole of date (IAU 1976).

In the 1984 through 2oo6 editions of The Astronomical Almanac,the polar motion was measured with respect to the celestial ephemeris pole,chosen to be along theaxis of figure for the mean surface of a model of Earth in which the free motions have zero amplitude.Prior to1984,the ephemeris pole waschosen to be the instantaneous axis of rotation.Atkinson (1973); Atkinson (1975),however,pointed out that use of the instantaneous pole of rotation led to quasi-diurnal motions,and thus,it was not the best reference pole.

# 6.8 Time and Earth Orientation

Time asmeasured by the Earth'srotation,may be thought of as the difference along the equator between the CRS(§ 6.2) and the projection of a terrestrial reference longitude onto the CRS.In the case of Universal Time,UT,the Earth'srotation is measured with respect to the mean fictitious anti-solardirection,midnight,and the terrestrial reference meridian is the origin of longitude of the TRS(S 6.2).For local meansolar time,the Earth's rotation is measured with respectto the mean fictitious anti-solar direction (midnight),and the reference meridian is the local meridian of longitude.For sidereal time theangle is measured with respectto the mean equinox for mean sidereal time andthe true equinox for apparent sidereal time.When thereference meridian for sidereal timeis the TRS,the sidereal time is either GreenwichMeanSidereal Time,GMST,orGreenwich ApparentSidereal Time,GAST.When thereference on the CRS is the CIO(§ 6.7)and the terrestrial reference meridian is the origin of longitude ofthe TRS,then theangulardifference is the Earth RotationAngle,ERA.

Until the nineteenth century the basic unit of time,the day,was based on the assumption that the Earth's rotation rate was constant,and,the determination of time depended on astronomical observations.There are seasonal differences in the apparent motion of the Sun that have been known since at least the time of Ptolemy.These differencesare not the result of variation in the Earth's rotation rate.They arise from the non-uniform motion of the Earth in its orbit about the Sun,and lead to the differentiation of apparent solar time and mean solar time.

The failure of successive lunar theories to represent the observed motion of the Moon eventuallyled to the suspicion that the rotation of the Earth was not uniform.In ephemerides calculated from gravitational theories,time isindependent of the variable rotation of the Earth.Consequently,the Earth's non-uniform rotation would cause the observed hour angle ofacelestial bodyto differfrom the position tabulated in the ephemeris.

Analysis of the discrepancies between observed and computed positions revealed the difference between the Earth's rotation and a uniform timescale.These discrepancies were most evident in the mean motion of the Moon,due to the accuracy with which the position can be observed and the rapidity of its motion.Similar discrepanciesin the motions ofother solar system bodies,in proportion to the magnitude of their mean motions,were also observed. These discrepanciesconfirmed the variable rateof theEarth'srotation.

The secular retardationof the rate of rotation due to tidal interaction between the Earth and Moon was the first variationto be recognized.Adams (1853) showed thatthe observed secular acceleration of the mean motionof theMooncould not totallybeproduced by gravitational perturbations.The validity of these results was hotly contested.The issue was resolved when Ferrell (1864) and Delaunay (1865) showed from dynamical principles that the tides exert a retarding action on the rotationof the Earth,accompaniedbyavariation ofthe orbital velocity of the Moon in accordance with the conservation of angular momentum.

Newcomb (1878)considered the possibility of irregular Variations of the Earth's rotation as an explanation oflunar residuals.He could not find confirmation from planetary data (Newcomb1912),however.Some of the difficulties faced by Newcomb,and prior astronomers, arose from inadequacies in the lunar theory.This situation was rectified when Brown (1919) introduced his new tables.A numberof studies offered increasing evidence of irregular variations inthe Earth's rotation.Most important were papersbyde Sitter(1927)and Spencer Jones (1939b),which correlated irregularities in the Moon's motion with irregularities of the inner planets.

Meanwhile,the accuracy of crystal-controlled clocks was becoming comparable with that of the rotation of the Earth.By comparing the observed rates of the clocks of national time services,Stoyko(1937) found a periodic seasonal variation in the rate of rotation.

TheEarth's rotationrate was eventually found to have secular,irregular,and periodic variations.Since mean solar time was determined directly from astronomical observations of the Earth's rotation,it could no longer be considered uniform.

Thus,a uniform timescale based on the motions of the bodies of the solar system, Ephemeris Time,ET,later followed by $\mathrm { T _ { e p h } }$ (the JPL realization of Barycentric Dynamical Time,TDB)replaced the rotationof the Earth as the independent variable for planetary ephemerides. ET and $\mathrm { T _ { e p h } }$ are discussed in Chapter 3.This section will discuss time as it relatestoEarth rotation thatis,solar time( $\left( \ S 6 . 8 . 1 \right)$ ,Universal Time(UT) see $\ S 6 . 8 . 3$ ,Earth Rotation Angle (ERA) see $\ S 6 . 8 . 4$ ,and sidereal time $( \ S 6 . 8 . 5 )$

# 6.8.1Apparent Solar Time,Mean Solar Time,and the Equationof Time

Apparent solar time is the measure of time defined by theactual diurnal motion of the Sun.If the Sun’s right ascension increased at a uniform rate and the Earth's rotation were uniform, solar days would be equal in length throughout the year,and apparent solar time would then provideauniform measure of time.However,like the Earth's rotation rate,the Sun's motion in right ascension is not uniform because the Sun moves along the ecliptic rather than the equator,and the Sun’s motionalong the ecliptic is not constant due to the Earth's orbit being elliptical,not circular.
