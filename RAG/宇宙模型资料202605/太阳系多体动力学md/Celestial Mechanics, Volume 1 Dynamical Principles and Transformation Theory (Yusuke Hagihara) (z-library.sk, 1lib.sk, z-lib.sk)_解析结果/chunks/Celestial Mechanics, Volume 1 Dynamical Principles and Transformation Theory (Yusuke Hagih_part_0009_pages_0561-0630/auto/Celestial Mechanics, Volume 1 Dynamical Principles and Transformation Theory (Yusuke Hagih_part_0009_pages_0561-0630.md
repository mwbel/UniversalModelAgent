In Section 4.10 we have shown that the order of the differential equations of the $\pmb { n }$ -bodyproblem can be reduced from $6 n$ to $6 n - 1 2$ by the use of Euler's classical ten integrals. Several attempts have been made to discover other integrals. Bruns (1887, 1887a) proved that there cannot be any integral that is algebraic with respect to the coordinates and momenta,other than the classical integrals.Poincaré (l889） further proved that there cannot be any integral that is uniform with respect to the Keplerian elements,other than the classical integrals.

We have shown in Sections 5.14 and 5.19 that the position coordinates and the momenta are uniform or regular, that is, single-valued and continuous,with respect to the osculating elements computed from these coordinates and momenta, except in the case of collisions of two or more bodies of the system.It will be shown in a later chapter that a similar result can be obtained on the solution itself of the equations of motion.This theorem of Poincaré has a part of its validity domain in common with the theorem of Bruns,although each has its own validity domain. The theorem of Poincaré presupposes that the right-hand members of the differential equations are developed in ascending powers of a small parameter $\mu$ ，described in Sections 5.16-5.20；that is,the disturbing mass must be assumed to be so small, that it is impossible to apply the result to the general $\pmb { n }$ -body problem- for example,to the system of three bodies with finite masses of comparable magnitude. These theorems become trivial in the case of two bodies,as the problem can then be solved by means of the classical integrals alone.
