# 14.16THE THIRD INTEGRAL

Contopoulos (1958,1960,1963) pointed out the existence of a third integral for the differential equations of motion of an individual star in a galactic system with a potential of a certain type.

Let $\pmb { I }$ be the integral in question and $I , I _ { i }$ be the complete set of independent integrals. Consider the space $\mathbf { \Phi } _ { I _ { i } } = a _ { i }$ $( i = 1 , 2 , \dotsc , p )$ ， where $a _ { i }$ are constants.The integral $\pmb { I }$ is said to isolate in this space if there exists at least one pair of sets $\boldsymbol { Q }$ ， $Q ^ { \prime }$ each with positive measure in this space, such that for all $P \in { \cal Q }$ ， $P ^ { \prime } \in Q ^ { \prime }$ we have $I ( P ) \neq I ( P ^ { \prime } )$ . If for almost all sets $( a _ { 1 } , a _ { 2 } , \ldots , a _ { p } )$ the integral $\pmb { I }$ does not isolate in the corresponding space $\bar { I } _ { i } = a _ { i }$ $( i = 1 , 2 , \dotsc , p )$ ， then $\pmb { I }$ is said to be non-isolating (Lynden-Bell, 1962;Wintner, 1947).

Recently Hénon and Heiles (1964) examined the existence of a third isolating integral of motion in an axially symmetric potential

$$
V ( x , y ) = { \textstyle { \frac { 1 } { 2 } } } ( x ^ { 2 } + y ^ { 2 } + 2 x ^ { 2 } y - { \textstyle { \frac { 2 } { 3 } } } y ^ { 3 } )
$$

by numerical experiments. We have the energy integral

$$
V ( x , y ) + { \textstyle \frac { 1 } { 2 } } ( \dot { x } ^ { 2 } + \dot { y } ^ { 2 } ) = E .
$$

A trajectory for a point in the phase space $( x , y , { \dot { x } } , { \dot { y } } )$ is plotted in a three-dimensional space $( x , y , \dot { y } )$ by the presence of the energy integral. The condition expressed by the inequality

$$
V ( x , y ) + \textstyle \frac { 1 } { 2 } \dot { y } ^ { 2 } \le E
$$

defines normally a bounded volume.If there is no other isolating integral,then the trajectory fills everywhere densely the volume defined by the inequality and is called ergodic (Sections 2.7,2.8,and 2.9). Consider the successive intersections of a trajectory with the plane $x = 0$ in the upward direction，that is,the successive points of the trajectory lying on the $( x , { \dot { y } } )$ -plane with $x = 0$ ， $\dot { x } > 0$ . In general, there will be an infinite sequence of such points,called successive consequents by Poincaré (1885a, 1899； Section 23.4). A transformation of a point of intersection to the next succeeding point of intersection is a kind of surface transformation (Section 25.18). An invariant point of this transformation corresponds to a periodic solution. The curve connecting the successive consequents is the projection on $x = 0$ of the trajectory.If there exists no isolating integral other than the known integrals, then these successive consequents fill an area $A : V ( 0 , y ) + \textstyle { \frac { 1 } { 2 } } \dot { y } ^ { 2 } \equiv E$ ， $x = 0$ ，of the surface $x = 0$ .But,if there exists another isolating integral,then such points lie on a curve on the surface.This is the method followed by Henon and Heiles for examining the existence of a third integral.

Consider a system with given energy and angular momentum. If the energy is small, then,according to Henon and Heiles,a third integral seems to exist.If the energy is higher than a certain critical energyin this case $E = 0$ —-then there are an infinite number of separated regions in the phase space where such a third integral still seems to exist. The space left free between these regions is the ergodic region, where the third integral is non-isolating.If the energy is further increased, then the proportion of allowable phase space occupied by this ergodic region increases very rapidly with the increasing energy and tends to occupy the whole space.

Contopoulos and Hadjidemetriou (1968） studied the intersections of the orbits in a three-dimensional subspace of the phase space for a two-dimensional potential corresponding to fixed values of the energy. They distinguished three types of orbits:(i) the isolating orbits whose intersection points lie on a smooth curve,which they call an invariant curve；(ii) the quasi-isolating or semi-ergodic orbits whose intersection points look as though they are scattered at random,filling parts of the available space, the limiting case being the ergodic orbits whose intersection points fill the whole available space ; and (ii) the escaping orbits, which appear only if the energy-integral surface extends to infinity.

Goudas and Barbanis (1963) showed that at the initial conditions of one family of periodic orbits of a system of differential equations, the third integral of Contopoulos (1960,1963 ；Barbanis,1962） and the Hamiltonian have collinear gradient to a fairly close approximation，and also that the third integral conserves its constant along the same family up to three significant figures, thus exhibiting the behavior of an approximate, time-independent, single-valued,and perhaps analytic,integral. But the analyticity may or may not be true for cases where the number $( A / B ) ^ { 1 / 2 }$ is rational, in which case a denumerably infinite number of vanishing denominators appear in the infinite series of the third integral and cause the divergence of the series. For irrational values of $( A / B ) ^ { 1 / 2 }$ ，small divisors occur and the possibility of convergence exists. Goudas (1968） showed， in accordance with Poincaré's theorem concerning the characteristic exponents in systems with multiple integrals (Section 19.2)， that the gradients of the Hamiltonian and the third integral are collinear along each member of a certain family of periodic orbits.In this family the constant of the third integral is conserved to about two or three significant figures ; but there is a new family of periodic orbits along each member of which the third integral is not conserved at all.

Bray and Goudas (1967) concluded that a new integral with a single-valued gradient cannot exist unless its gradient is collinear to the gradient of the energy integral along all singular closed solutions. Ordinary solutions,on the other hand,have never been found after extensive search by many investigators ；thus the conclusion is that general stable members of low inclination in the cases of $L _ { 2 }$ and $L _ { 3 }$ in the restricted three-body problem (Section 18.22) indeed exist.They discussed the question of a possible new integral in the neighborhood of these points.

Contopoulos and Barbanis (1968） presented numerical evidence that,although the third integral is tangent to the Hamiltonian along some periodic orbits as has been shown by Goudas (1968),it is not tangent along nonperiodic orbits,and accordingly that it is not a function of the Hamiltonian.The set of periodic orbits is probably dense in general,but a given form of the third integral is valid in the neighborhood of a limited number of these orbits,and no form of the third integral is valid for all periodic orbits except in integrable cases.
