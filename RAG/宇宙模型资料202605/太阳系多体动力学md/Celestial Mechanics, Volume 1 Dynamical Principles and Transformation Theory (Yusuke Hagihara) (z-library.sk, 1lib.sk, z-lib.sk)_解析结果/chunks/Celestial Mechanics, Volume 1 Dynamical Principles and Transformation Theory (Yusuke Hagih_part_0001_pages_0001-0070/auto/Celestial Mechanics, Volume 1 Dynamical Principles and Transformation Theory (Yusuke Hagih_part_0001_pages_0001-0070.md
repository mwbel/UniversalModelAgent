# Preface

The rather ambitious aim of the present series is to recapitulate the results of the whole field of celestial mechanics and the associated branches of science during the last hundred years.During this period the classical method of formal expansion into series, due to Laplace and Le Verrier, has been severely criticized;a new class of orbits called periodic solutions has been introduced, with applications to Hil's lunar theory. The trigonometric series employed in the ordinary perturbation theory are expanded in powers of the small quantities characteristic ofactual natural bodies,and with arguments proportional to time. Bruns and Poincaré have proved that such series are not uniformly convergent,although the existence of the solution has been established on function-theoretic grounds by Sundman. The newly discovered class of functions called “quasi-periodic" and“almost periodic"’ can be expanded in similar but uniformly convergent form. The question naturally arises: What conditions should be satisfied by the equations of motion of the $\boldsymbol { n }$ -body problem? There has been some endeavor to solve these equations by resorting to integral equations and infinite nonlinear analysis. The question of stability of motion, especially of the solar system,has been discussed by means of the linearized system of equations according to the ideas of Poincaré and Liapounov. The problem is to study stability behavior by means of nonlinear equations, even by dealing with nonconvergent expansions in the vicinity of the equilibrium point.Birkhoff has extended the idea of periodic solutions of Hill and Poincaré to recurrent motions,central motions,and wandering motions,on the basis of measure theory.Another important aspect of the modern trend in celestial mechanics is the topological theory initiated by Poincaré and

Birkhoff, for example the invariant-point theorem and the ergodic theorem. The present series ofvolumes undertakes the program oftreating all such questions, extending over the whole of celestial mechanics and associated fields, and of giving suggestions for future development of the subject,stimulated by the launching ofartificial celestial bodies.This first volume presents introductory dynamical principles as Part I, and transformation theory as Part II.

Part I begins with the principles of analytical dynamics.From the variational principle of Hamilton, and using the calculus of variations, we deduce Hamilton's canonical form ofthe differential equations. As an introduction to the topological study of dynamical trajectories, Riemannian geometry is described as it pertains to the quadratic form of the kinetic energy. The notions ofinvariance, covariance,and contravariance are introduced for use in dealing with the canonical transformations. The Hamilton-Jacobi partial differential equation is derived both from the canonical equations and from a hydrodynamical analogy.Liouville's and Stäckel’s theorems are proved,and the condition for separability of the variables in the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is obtained by using Riemannian geometry; the theorems obtained are also applied to the Schrodinger equation in wave mechanics.

Chapter 2 deals with quasi-periodic motions.We define a libration,a revolution,and an asymptotic motion for a simple integrable dynamical system; these definitions serve as keys to much more complicated types of motion in celestial mechanics.The multiply periodic motions and conditionally periodic motions are discussed on the basis of Weierstrass's preparatory theorem in the theory of functions of many variables and Kronecker's theorem in number theory. The motion of molecules in a cubic vessel is solved as a concrete example of ergodicity. Bohl's quasiperiodic functions and Bohr's almost periodic functions are defined, and certain of their properties which will be useful later are stated. Schwarzschild's relativistic one-body problem in the gravitational field and the problem of the motion of an earth satellite under the action of the distorted earth are solved by using the Hamilton-Jacobi equation and quasi-periodic motions.Various other methods of treating such problems are subjects of the subsequent volumes.The theory of algebraic integrals is briefly sketched as an introduction to the ideas used in the text. Chapter 3 is dedicated to particular solutions of the three-body and many-body problems.Euler'sand Lagrange's types ofparticular solutions are obtained for the $\pmb { n }$ -body problem in a general manner, and the nature of the motion is fully analyzed.The isosceles-triangular solution is discussed on the basis of the theory ofanalytic functions.

Part II of this volume begins in Chapter 4 with Lie's theory of continuous groups of transformations, with application to the $\pmb { n }$ -bodyproblem.The theorems due to Poisson,Liouville,and Lie are proved on the basis of Lie's ideas of complete systems, involution,Lie's function groups and distinguished functions； then these theorems are applied to the integrals of the $\pmb { n }$ -body problem. Integral invariants and adiabatic invariants are introduced for the theoretical reduction of the order of the differential equations.The existence of adiabatic invariants is proved in a general abstract space for use in later arguments.A summary of the modern theory of Lie groups is given with a view to suggesting a new trend of development.

In Chapter 5,the differential equations of the $\pmb { n }$ -bodyproblem are reduced by using the known integrals.By Lagrange's, Levi-Civita's, and Poincaré's transformations, the differential equations are reduced to the lowest order.The two-body problem, especially,is discussed in full; and various expansions are obtained, using the theory of functions as applied to Keplerian motion to establish the convergence of the expansions.Then the equations for the variation of elements,Delaunay's,Poincaré's, and Kepler's,are derived as the fundamental principle in perturbation theory. The role of the angular-momentum integral.is stressed in view of the elimination of the node by Jacobi. Various kinds of canonical variables are defined, along with their dynamical meanings.

Chapter 6 is devoted to discussions ofBruns's and Poincare's theorems. The generality ofBruns's proof of the theorem of the nonexistence of new algebraic integrals is extended from three bodies to $_ { n }$ bodies.The proof of Painleve's extension of Bruns's theorem is given.The integrals of the restricted three-body problem are discussed with special reference to the ergodic theorem.Poincaré's theorem concerning the nonexistence of new uniform integrals is proved and discussed in full detail. Exceptions to Poincaré's theorem are also dealt with.The top motions of Euler, Lagrange,and Kowalewski are discussed from the point of view of Bruns's and Poincaré's theorems,and the solution,given with integrals, is based on the theory of algebraic functions, especially on the Riemann $\vartheta .$ -functions and the hyperelliptic functions.

Volume II,which will be published shortly after Volume I, will contain perturbation theories in classical celestial mechanics for the motions of planets,asteroids, satellites, comets, and the moon, that is, theories of Laplace, Lagrange,Delaunay, von Zeipel, Hansen,Gylden, Hill, Brown,and others,and their applications to the motions of artifcial celestial bodies. Questions will be raised on the gaps in the asteroidal distribution and on the capture of comets.

In Volume III, the form of the solutions of these perturbation theories and the convergence of the series expansions for the solutions will be discussed on mathematical grounds. The question of the third integral of motion will be touched upon.Differential equations with periodic coefficients and with quasi-periodic coefficients will be used in connection with perturbation theory.Krylov-Bogoliubov's averaging method and Diliberto's periodic surface theory will be applied to the motion of celestial bodies.

In Volume IV,Poincaré's theory of periodic solutions will be discussed in full with various modern versions of the theory and with practical applications to the three- and many-body problems.Periodic solutions will be dealt with on the basis ofWintner's nonlinear infinite analysis and Lichtenstein's nonlinear integral equations.Motions in the neighborhood of an equilibrium point will be discussed according to the theories of Birkhoff and Siegel. The conditional existence of quasi-periodic solutions in the manner of Kolmogorov, Arnold, and Moser will be shown. The stability theory of Liapounov and its modern development will be described.

Volume V will contain proofs of the existence of the solution of the three-body problem by Sundman,Levi-Civita,Chazy,and Merman,as well as the proof of the rigorous capture theory of Merman; finally, there will be discussed the topological theory of Poincaré and Birkhoff, including the theory of characteristics,manifolds of motion，surface transformations, the invariant-point theorem, the ergodic theorem,and almost periodic motions.

# Introduction

Celestial mechanics, founded by Laplace, has been the most elegant of the mathematical sciences.It is a typically deductive science, treating the dynamical characteristics -that is, the motion and the figure of celestial bodies-by pure mathematical deduction based on the laws of dynamics. According to Poincaré, celestial mechanics convinces us of the existence of the laws of nature and teaches us how infinitely accurate those laws are.Until the present century,mathematical analysis and celestial mechanics progressed side by side,stimulating and helping each other, ever since the discovery of differential and integral calculus by Newton. Recent developments in technology now facilitate computation of the ephemerides of celestial bodies by means of electronic computers. The whole program of planning the orbits of artificial satellites,and the tracking of such satellites, relies at present on such machine computation. The three-body problem and also the $\pmb { n }$ -bodyproblem may be solved numerically; for example, we may solve numerically dynamical systems consisting of lOO or 2OO stars in stellar clusters,and verify their global characteristics derived from statistical mechanics.

At this stage of the science we are compelled to discuss the foundations of the methods of celestial mechanics. The value of fundamental theory lies in its potential not only for enabling us to reflect on the processes and methods we have pursued and to attach physical meaning to the labyrinth of mere numbers,but also helping us to comprehend their merits and demerits, for steering us toward a completely harnessed logical system of theories.It is the aim of the present series of volumes to examine on rigorous mathematical grounds the question of whether the deductive process adopted in celestial mechanics can be logically justified.

Before a theory is fully justified on logical grounds,its results cannot safely be compared with observations in order to verify the laws of nature.For example, there is the great question whether the Newtonian laws,with their Galilean notion of time and space,or the Einsteinian relativistic theories of time, space,and gravitation are the right ones; the question, that is, whether and how we should amend our notion of time, space,and gravitation and finally our ultimate world-picture.

The motion of two bodies attracting each other by the Newtonian law of gravitation has been completely solved. There are three degrees of freedom and the differential equations of motion are integrated with six constants of integration. The orbit is known to be a conic section on a single plane and the motion is Keplerian.The usual method is to take as the integration constants: (l), the inclination of the orbital plane; (2), the longitude of the node; (3), the semimajor axis; (4),the eccentricity; (5),the longitude of the pericenter;and (6),the epoch of, say,the pericenter passage. These six constants are called the Keplerian elements.

The problem of computing the motion of three bodies attracting each other by the Newtonian law of gravitation,with arbitrary masses and arbitrary initial conditions,is called the three-body problem.Its fame is due to the difficulty of solving it: the labors of many eminent mathematicians in the past have borne hardly any fruit in this respect. Two kinds of particular solution have been obtained through the discoveries of Euler andLagrange in the case ofarbitrary masses: the straight-line equilibrium solution and the equilateral-triangular equilibrium solution.In the case of two equal masses we have the isosceles-triangular solution.These are the only exact solutions ever discovered.

An elementary method for solving a system of differential equations is to find out as many independent integrals as the order of the differential equations. The twelve classical integrals are: one integral for the energy, three for the angular momentum, six for the center of mass— these ten are called the classical Eulerian integrals 一and finally one integral (discovered by Jacobi) for the elimination of the node and one for the elimination of time.Thus,by using these twelve integrals,the order of the differential equations for the n-body problem is reduced from 6n to $6 n - 1 2$ . Lie's theory of continuous groups of transformations, in particular the ideas of contact transformations, Pfaffians, involution, function groups,and distinguished functions, has been applied to reduce the order ofthe given differential equations, and hence to transform them to those ofa lower order. The theory has revealed no means for the further reduction of the order. On the other hand, Bruns proved (and the proof has been extended since by Painleve) the nonexistence offurther integrals which are algebraic in the momenta；and Poincaré proved the nonexistence offurther integrals which are uniformin the Keplerian elements. An exception to Poincaré's theorem is Kowalewski's top motion,which can be solved explicitly by means of hyperelliptic functions.Thus we must abandon our previous attempts to solve the $\pmb { n } \cdot \pmb { n } \cdot \pmb { n } .$ -body problem by the elementary method of finding further independent integrals and have recourse to some transcendental procedure.Recently the existence of the third integral has been under discussion with regard to the stellar dynamics of clusters and galaxies.The integral as well as the adelphic integral of Whittaker is nonuniform and its existence does not invalidate Poincare's theorem.

The question whether there exists a solution ofthe differential equations for the three-body problem has been settled completely by Sundman. The differential equations for the motion become singular at a collision of two of the bodies or at a simultaneous colision of the three bodies. Painlevé has shown the condition for a collision to be transcendental. The condition was explicitly deduced by Bisconcini and Levi-Civita before Sundman.A simultaneous collision of three bodies occurs when and only when the total angular momentum of the three-body system is zero,as was proved by Sundman. Sundman regularized the differential equations in the sense of Klein and Koebe and proved that the coordinates and the momenta of the three bodies are expanded in ascending powers of the variable $\tau = ( t - t _ { 0 } ) ^ { 2 / 3 }$ ，where $t _ { 0 }$ denotes the time of the collsion.Recently several methods for the regularization have been proposed, particularly for numerical solutions of the problem.Thus the existence of the solution has been proved in the sense of the Weierstrassian theory of analytic functions and, indeed, both before and after the collision； that is, the analytic continuation of the solution has been proved possible beyond the instant of the collision.We cannot lay too much stress on the importance of this theorem by Sundman.It is the basis of the topological study of the trajectories for an infinite time interval. This result has been extended to the $\pmb { n }$ -body problem.The behavior of the motion at infinity has also been studied by Chazy and recently by Merman.The theorem by Poincaré,Schwarzschild,von Zeipel, and Chazy regarding the impossibility of a capture has been challenged by Merman as to its rigor for justifying some of the cosmogonical hypotheses on the origin of the solar system.

As has been stated,we must at the present stage rely on some transcendental process for the solution of the three-body problem.The process presently employed is the iteration of successve approximations, the so-called theory of perturbations.A primitive procedure for computing the perturbations is to take the Keplerian motion as a frst approximation by supposing the third body to be ofsmall mass or to be at a large distance (referring to the actual configuration of the solar system).The result is expanded in powers of the disturbing mass Then the Keplerian elements are no longer constants but vary slowly, the rate of variation depending on the disturbing mass. We determine the variation of these elements by solving the differential equations that define their variations with time. This method is called the uariation of elements of Lagrange and was first applied to the motion of the major planets byLe Verrier.Theories of the motion of the planets in the solar system are founded not only on such expansions in powers of the disturbing masses but also on expansions in powers of the eccentricities and orbital inclinations,which are in fact small owing to the present configuration of the solar system. Naturally, the convergent nature of these expansions is essential for the practical success of the theory of the motion of the particular planet in question. However, the new development of artificial celestial bodies compels us to make a fresh start, to investigate methods of completely new character, since the eccentricities and the orbital inclinations are not so very small.

Such a method of formal integration fails for $k _ { 1 } n _ { 1 } + k _ { 2 } n _ { 2 } = 0$ The terms for which $k _ { 1 } = k _ { 2 } = 0$ are called secular. The terms for which $k _ { 1 } n _ { 1 } + k _ { 2 } n _ { 2 } = 0$ without $k _ { 1 } = k _ { 2 } = 0$ are called critical. In such cases terms multiplied by $t$ in front of the trigonometric function appear. The sum $k _ { 1 } n _ { 1 } + k _ { 2 } n _ { 2 }$ can be made as small as we please according to Kronecker's theorem in number theory by making the integers $k _ { 1 } , \ k _ { 2 }$ sufficiently large. A small divisor $k _ { 1 } n _ { 1 } + k _ { 2 } n _ { 2 }$ appears then in the denominator in the above method of integration.The period of such a term is very long- ${ 2 \pi } / ( { k _ { 1 } n _ { 1 } } + { k _ { 2 } n _ { 2 } } )$ —and the term is said to be of long period.If such a circumstance exists in a term of comparatively lower degree in the eccentricities and the inclinations一especially when the mean motions of the two planets are nearly in commensurable ratio of a simple fraction- then the magnitude of the coefficient of the term becomes large and the series obtained by such formal integration looks as though it were divergent. This is due to the inadequacy of the formal integration procedure.

It was by such a method that Poisson proved the invariability of the semimajor axes as regards the secular perturbation of the second order; and on such a method Laplace and Lagrange based the theory of secular perturbations by showing that they are formally represented by trigonometric series of very long periods. This idea has been extended by Newcomb,and the solution of the differential equations of the planetary theory is proved to be represented by formal trigonometric series having linear functions of $t$ as arguments and coefficients proceeding in powers of the eccentricities, of the inclinations, and (above all) of the disturbing masses.Expansions of the solution in series of similar forms have been obtained by Lindstedt,Bohlin,Gyldén,and others.Unfortunately,as Poincaré proved, such series are not uniformly convergent.Hence they cannot by any means represent the real solution of the problem,although they may represent the solution in a certain degree of approximation within present-day accuracy of the observations during a limited time interval. Such series were called semiconvergent by Poincaré and may be useful for practical purposes. It is essential,at the present stage,to be informed of the accuracy of the approximation within a certain finite time interval by means of such semiconvergent series as compared with the accuracy of observing techniques. It may happen that the series, when truncated at a certain term,represents at present the solution within an error negligible compared with observational errors; in that event, it might deviate more and more when truncated at a higher-order term in order to obtain more accurate values, similarly to Stirling's asymptotic formula for a factorial. Indeed, the accuracy of the observing technique is rapidly progressing. Thus,we are confronted with urgent need for new approaches to a more accurate solution of the problem. The averaging method due to Krylov and Bogoliubov, originally invented for use in electrical engineering and now in use for the motion of artificial celestial bodies,may enable us to estimate the error of approximations.The case in which the mean motions are commensurable has been worked out. The method ofBirkhoff, since discussed by Siegel and Moser,is applicable to the estimation of the motions in the neighborhood of an equilibrium point in a dynamical system.Ritz-Galerkin'sapproximation method might be considered in this respect. Two-variable asymptotic expansion technique and polynomial expansion procedure are being used in computer programming of the motion of space vehicles.It is remarked here that the existence of mean motions in the Laplace-Lagrange theory of secular perturbation has been a topic of interest in mathematics recently. Weyl, following Bohl, has completed the proof in a mathematical sense on the basis of probability distributions,and Bohr and Jessen on the theory of almost periodic functions.

Trigonometric series with linear functions of time as the arguments, when they are uniformly convergent, represent quasi-eriodic functions in the sense of Bohl and Esclangon. They belong to a class of functions named almost periodic by Bohr. If the solution of the planetary motion were known to satisfy the condition for almost periodicity, then the series formally obtained in the ordinary perturbation theory would be uniformly convergent.Gylden has discussed the probability for an arbitrary number to be rational and argued that the series obtained were convergent except for a set of measure zero.But Poincaré,after Bruns, proved that they are not uniformly convergent. The Borel series and the Liouville numbers play an important role in this problem.It is shown that the series is divergent if $\displaystyle n _ { 1 } : n _ { 2 }$ in the foregoing formulas is a Liouville's number or if it is a $U .$ -number of Mahler. Petersson and Siegel recently tried to refine the proof of the nonconvergence of the series. Indeed the planetary motion does not always satisfy the condition for almost periodicity.Hence we can say nothing about the physical characteristics of the motion in a very long time interval,both in the past and in the future,unless the idea of the measure theory is introduced and the cases of Lebesgue measure zero are ignored,as has been done by Arnold. The idea of Kolmogorov and Arnold is that if the ratio $\boldsymbol { n } _ { 1 } : \boldsymbol { n } _ { 2 }$ satisfies certain inequalities,then the perturbed motion can be represented by quasiperiodic functions and is everywhere dense on the distorted torus obtained (by the Newtonian approximation process to avoid the appearance of small divisors） from the torus for the nonperturbed system.

In the actual motion in the solar system the mean motions of Jupiter and Saturn are nearly in the commensurable ratio 5:3 and those of Uranus and Neptune nearly 2:1. There are corresponding terms with long periods,which are collectively called the great inequality, of com-paratively large amplitude.We find gaps in the distribution of the mean motions of asteroids in commensurable ratios to that of Jupiter, for example,2:1,and a clustering tendency a little off from such exact commensurability. These are called Kirkwood's gaps. For certain commensurable ratios, such as 3:2 and 1:1, there are asteroids at nearly exactly commensurable mean motions.This state of affairs cannot be explained by the presence of small divisors in the formal series solution; indeed, it is one of the unsolved enigmas in celestial mechanics.It is here that we urgently need mathematical discussion, based on purely logical foundations, of the nature of our formal method of integration. There are also several cases of commensurable ratios of the mean motions in satellite systems. Several gaps appear in Saturn's rings. The inner three of the four Galilean satellites of Jupiter are most remarkable. Their motions are very complicated and interesting. Using the latest developments ofradar technique, work on the resonance problem in the rotation of planets, besides the new determination of astronomical constants, is now progressing.

The usual method for computing the perturbations fails in such cases. We have several methods for computing the ephemerides of such planets and satellites.Hansen,Delaunay,Newcomb,Gylden,Brown,and von Zeipel each invented his own method.Von Zeipel's method is currently applied to the motion of artificial and even natural celestial bodies. Yet the series obtained by these methods are not uniformly convergent. The methods have been applied to the motion of Jupiter and Saturn, of Uranus and Neptune,and of some of the asteroids having mean motions nearly commensurable with that of Jupiter.Gyldén's heuristic method is worth noting for its originality.Instead of taking the Keplerian motion as the first approximation, he took as his intermediary orbit the absolute orbit with moving perihelion obtained by dropping all short-period terms from the perturbations. He integrated the equations by means of elliptic functions and thought that the solution written in series in the theory of elliptic functions was convergent.But Poincaré proved that these series,too,are not uniformly convergent.Bohlin expanded the solution of the general three-body problem in a different kind of series and called the method autologous.

A revolutionary trend in celestial mechanics was inaugurated toward the end of last century by Hillin his theory of the motion of the moon. Hill took as his intermediary orbit a periodic orbit ofa fictitious, though very nearly true, system of the sun, moon, and earth. Hill's theory has been completed by Brown for the actual numerical calculation of the lunar ephemerides.A linear differential equation with periodic coefficients and a determinant with an infinite number of rows and columns play important roles in Hil's theory.The series Hill obtained for this periodic orbit are uniformly convergent (the radius of convergence has been derived on the basis of nonlinear infinite analysis), but the series repre-senting the deviation of the actual orbit from this intermediary orbit are not uniformly convergent.This ingenious idea of Hill's was generalized by Poincaré in his theory of periodic solutions and has been applied to the motion of those asteroids with mean motions nearly commensurable with that of the main disturbing body,Jupiter,and also to the motion of the Galilean satellites of Jupiter by de Sitter. Poincarés theory of periodic solutions has been cultivated mathematically by Perron, Lefschetz,Siegel,and others in conjunction with the theory of differential equations. Numerical computations for periodic solutions of idealized systems have been worked out by Darwin, Hill,and Stromgren, among others,and are now in progress with the help of electronic computers. Indeed, following Stromgren， Henon，among others, has made with computers a complete survey of orbits in the restricted three-body problem (in which the second finite mass rotates around the first finite mass in a circular orbit and the third mass is negligible) and studied the genealogy of the periodic solutions. The existence of horseshoe periodic orbits in the equilateral-triangular case of this problem, as conjectured by Brown,is now cleared up by means of computers.It is remarkable that even the analytic manipulation of multiple Fourier series can now be achieved by the proper use ofcomputers. Poincaré's theory ofperiodic and asymptotic solutions can be discussed on the basis of the theory of nonlinear integral equations developed by Schmidt， Lichtenstein，and Wintner.The theory of bifurcation can be founded on this consolidated ground.

What is then the right form of the solution of the three-body problem, in particular of the planetary theory? The solutions so far obtained are all in trigonometric series with linear functions of time as arguments.As has been remarked, such series are not uniformly convergent and hence they are not the mathematical solution of the problem. If they were uniformly convergent, then the motion represented by such forms of the solution would be the superposition of periodic motions; hence the present configuration of the solar system would remain essentially unaltered for an unlimited time- that is, the system would be stable.At this stage we might ask: Is it not possible to try the solution in series proceeding in some sort of eigenfunctions,with due care for the nature ofthe singularities of the solution,on a function-theoretical basis? Owing to the lack of such work, we are not in a position to say anything about the stability of the motion in a rigorous sense.

The stability of a periodic solution has been discussed by Poincaré on the basis of his characteristic exponents, that is,by studying the behavior of nearby motions on the basis of the linearized equations for the variations of the generating periodic solutions.Solutions close to the particular solutions of the three-body problem and to the motion of particles forming the Saturnian rings have been discussed in this manner. Poisson's theorem on the invariability of the major axes in planetary motion,and the Laplace-Lagrange theory of secular perturbation establish in a certain sense the stability of the solar system.Liapounov has defined stability in a more rigorous mathematical sense. For the complete discussion of the nonlinear equations we must refer to topological studies. The criterion for stability is that of Lagrange,and the condition derived from the discussion of small deviations is one of the necessary conditions but not a suffcient condition.In the theory of the figures of a rotating liquid mass, we must refer to the energy criterion.Liapounov, Poincaré, and Schwarzschild discussed the condition for stability by developing the idea of Dirichlet. Liapounov's direct method is now greatly cultivated in theories and in application to control and optimization problems as well as topological dynamics. Search for the converse ofLiapounov's theorems has led to various refined ideas on stability, even in abstract topological spaces.

Since the formal series obtained for the solution ofour problem are not uniformly convergent, we can draw no conclusion as to the physical behavior of the motion merely on the basis of such formal series expansions.They may well be valid in a limited time interval within observational accuracy. Sundman's proof of the existence of the solution is based on the Weierstrassian theory of analytic functions,and the radius of convergence of his series is so small that nothing can be said about the physical characteristics of the motion,although the radius ofconvergence of Sundman's series as evaluated by Belorizky has been enlarged recently by reference to the polynomial expansion theory of arbitrary functions.

Thus the topological study of the trajectories in the manifold of motion was initiated by Poincaré.He discussed in the large the geometrical behavior of the solution curves of a nonlinear differential equation in the manifold of motion of any connectivity and with any Betti and torsion numbers,without reference to these series expansions,and hence built up his whole theory of topology.Hadamard,Poincaré,and Morse discussed the topological characteristics of geodesics on a surface, since a dynamical system devoid ofany external force field can be mapped on a curved surface of a suitable number of dimensions.It is remarkable that Denjoy found for the characteristics on the surface of a torus the de-pendence of the ultimate behavior of a dynamical system on the degree of regularity of the functions that characterize the motion. Poincare's theory on the existence of periodic solutions by means of the minimizing principle has been generalized by Birkhoff to the minimax principle and by Morse to functional topology and the calculus of variations in the large. Birkhoff applied the idea of Poincaré to the topological study of the manifold of motion in the restricted three-body problem.Poincare enunciated his famous invariant-point theorem, proved later by Birkhoff and extended by Brouwer,Birkhoff,and Nielsen.Poincaré and Birkhoff both applied the theorem to the proof of the existence of periodic orbits in the three-body problem.Birkhoff further studied the stability characters of the trajectories in the neighborhood of an invariant point of the surface transformations by using formal expansions.

Poincaré's idea of periodic motions has been extended by Birkhoff to recurrent motions, nonwandering motions,and central motions based on the point-set theory ofCantor.Poincaré proved his recurrence theorem which states that any trajectory can pass as near as we please to its initial position an infinite number of times if the domain of the trajectories is always bounded and if there exists a positive integral invariant such as the volume of the domain in the phase space. Consider a point set $\pmb { \cal E }$ which is invariant for a transformation $_ T$ in a manifold of motion $\Sigma$ .If there exists no measurable invariant set $\boldsymbol { \varepsilon }$ whose measure in the sense of Lebesgue is smaller than the measure of $\Sigma$ and different from zero, then the transformation is said to be metrically transitive.Such a motion has been studied by using modular groups, Fuchsian groups,and general topological groups.The recurrence theorem of Poincaré has been refined with the idea of point-set theory by Carathéodory,Khintchine, von Neumann, Birkhoff, Hopf, Wiener,and others to the modern ergodic theorem, the idea of mixture, homogeneous chaos,and statistical regularity.The ergodic theorem is closely related to the Markoff chain in the theory of probability, as Poincaré's recurrence theorem first appeared in his lectures on probability. It is the foundation of the kinetic theory of gases.

The trend of celestial mechanics at this stage of machine computers should be directed toward studying the general behavior of trajectories on rigorous mathematical foundations,so as to check the results of numerical computation,and at the same time toward estimating the errors involved in using nonconvergent series expansions within the accuracy to be expected from the limited observation time.Thus the science of celestial mechanics is now confronting a new revolutionary era by abruptly moving from reliance on the old weapon of series expansions toward rigorous mathematical discussion of the physical behavior of the trajectories for an unlimited time.The artificial satellites and planets are offering us the more difficult and more general problems of studying motions widely different from those of natural objects hitherto discussed in celestial mechanics. This new aspect completely surpasses the apparent elegance of the old-fashioned celestial mechanics,and its importance cannot be overemphasized.
