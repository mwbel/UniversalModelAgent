We have given in Section 6.l2 an exceptional case where there exists an algebraic integral in the restricted three-body problem.When two of the three bodies in the three-body problem collide at a finite distance at the end of a finite interval of time, the differential equations cease to be regular,as will be discussed later.Otherwise, the coordinates of the three bodies can be determined uniquely by Cauchy's theorem on the existence of the solution of a differential equation,and the solution can be expanded in integral powers of $t \mathrm { ~ - ~ } t _ { 0 }$ around an arbitrary initial condition for $t = t _ { 0 }$ (Painlevé, l897).There are two independent conditions for such a collision,which reduce to one in the case of the plane problem.The conditions for a collision in the two-body problem are algebraic, but the conditions for the three- and $\pmb { n }$ -body problems are transcendental according to Painlevé (1897，1900，i900a，1900b). Consider the state of motion for $t \to \pm \infty$ . Three of these four subspaces contain the region $h = 0$ in their interiors.Each orbit in these subspaces is elliptic-hyperbolic； two of the mutual distances become infinite of order l with regard to $t$ as $t \to \pm \infty$ ; the third mutual distance, which remains the same all the time,is bounded.The three mutual distances are either bounded or oscillatory inside the subspace bounded by three analytic surfaces.

When the three mutual distances become infinite, of order $\frac { 2 } { 3 }$ ，1,1, respectively, as $t$ increases indefinitely, the motion is said to be parabolichyperbolic.When all three mutual distances become infinite of order 1, the motion is said to be hyperbolic. When the three bodies collide at infinity, the order of infinity is $\scriptstyle { \frac { 2 } { 3 } }$ and the motion is said to be parabolic. The configuration of the three bodies in the immediate neighborhood of a simultaneous collision is that of either the equilateral triangular solution of Lagrange or the straight-line solution of Euler,as has been remarked in Chapter 3.In the first two cases the point at infinity $t = \infty$ is a transcendental singular point,but in the case of a colision it is an algebraic singularity of order two. These are the cases when the total energy constant $h$ of the whole system is positive.

The trajectories should be inside a subspace of eight dimensions embedded in the twelve-dimensional space, but in the case of a collision they should be inside a six-dimensional subspace. Two conditions are necessary for a collision and they have been shown (Chazy, l9l8,1922, 1929,1932,1933) to be transcendental with regard to $y _ { 1 } , y _ { 2 } , y _ { 3 } , y _ { 4 } , y _ { 5 } , y _ { 6 }$ The region in which the trajectories of the three bodies lie is divided into five regions,except for a simultaneous collision of the three bodies. The exterior region corresponds to hyperbolic motions,three of the five regions to ellptic-hyperbolic motions,and the remaining ffth either to oscillatory motions for which the three mutual distances are all bounded or to motions for which two of the mutual distances are arbitrarily large while the third is bounded. There are motions with elliptic osculating orbits in the fifth region.As $t$ increases indefinitely, each of the functions $y _ { 1 } , y _ { 2 } , y _ { 3 } , y _ { 4 } , y _ { 5 } , y _ { 6 }$ tends to a finite limit.The limits are uniform functions of the values of the twelve variables $x _ { i } , y _ { i }$ $( i = 1 , 2 , . . . , 6 )$ for a certain epoch.Hence, the squares of the sum and the difference of the limiting values of each of these six $y _ { i }$ are two uniform integrals in the domain of hyperbolic motions；each of those integrals represents a family of surfaces,and one of them passes through an arbitrary point in that domain. In the domain of elliptic-hyperbolic motions we denote by $y _ { 4 } , y _ { 5 } , y _ { 6 }$ the momentum of the third body (receding to infinity) relative to the center of mass of the two bodies remaining in a bounded finite domain,and define the eliptic elements by six out of the twelve osculating elements and the hyperbolic elements by the remaining six; then these elements tend to finite limits in each of the trajectories,and each of them defines a uniform integral in the corresponding domain. Those uniform integrals that can exist simultaneously in both of these two domains define a family of surfaces,only one of which can pass through an arbitrary point in the whole domain. Among these surfaces, the surface bounding one of the five domains is a particular one. It can be shown that the integrals corresponding to this bounding surface cannot be reduced to the energy integral or the angular-momentum integrals (see Chapter 22).

As will be discussed later in detail, this theorem of Poincaré deals with uniform integrals with respect to the twelve elements belonging to the two osculating elliptic motions. Such integrals are uniform in the domain of another two-dimensional space and do not enter in this theorem of Poincaré.

The theorem of Bruns, on the other hand, differs from the theorem of Poincaré and can be applied to such integrals. One such integral is neither an algebraic combination of the classical integrals,nor an algebraic combination of $y _ { i }$ only,as in the extension of the theorem by Painlevé.The bounding surfaces of the five domains of these trajectories are not algebraic surfaces.

Painlevé (1900,1900a,1900b) studied the cases in which the solution can be represented by meromorphic functions of $t$ with polar singularities. He has given algebraic differential equations that admit uniform integrals, besides the known functions, such as exponential, elliptic,Abelian,and uniform integrals of a linear differential equation including Fuchsian and automorphic functions. The critical points are called fixed if they do not depend on the integration constants; otherwise they are called movable. Painlevé,extending the works of Poincaréand Picard,examined differential equations that admit the general integrals only with fixed critical points.He considered differential equations of the first, second,and third order. In the case of the third order he found a new class of singularities, for example, those with a perfect set of discontinuities (see Chapter 22).

Arnold (963) recently solved the problem by means of quasi-periodic functions, along with the difficulty of the degenerate cases, making the severe assumption that the series representing the functions are almost everywhere convergent-by excluding the domain of Lebesgue measure zero, as will be discussed in a later chapter.

In the light of modern developments in wave mechanics and quantum mechanics，especially the theory based on Bohr's complementarity principle, our mode of thinking has changed. However,even now it is evident that only a first approximation is treated in the computation of the perturbation; indeed, the method for the second approximation fails completely owing to the nonconvergence of the process. We are in quantum mechanics confronted with the same grave difficulty of nonconvergence that makes its first appearance in classical dynamics.

\~ \mathsf { s n } \left( u + u ^ { \prime } \right) = \frac { \mathsf { s n } ~ u \mathrm { c n } ~ u ^ { \prime } \mathrm { d n } ~ u ^ { \prime } + \mathsf { s n } ~ u ^ { \prime } \mathrm { c n } ~ u \mathrm { d n } ~ u } { 1 - k ^ { 2 } \mathsf { s n } ^ { 2 } u \mathsf { s n } ^ { 2 } u ^ { \prime } } .
$$

This second solution of the inversion problem is due to Konigsberger.

Unfortunately this solution of the inversion problem is of little use in conjunction with the separation of variables in the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, except in the case of Kowalewski's top motion.

# Retrospect of Volume I

At this stage let us recapitulate the results of our discussion.

The elementary method for solving a system of differential equations of order $_ { 6 n }$ for the $_ { n }$ -body problem lies in the discovery of as many independent integrals as the order of the differential equations. So far, the known classical integrals are: one for energy， three for angular momentum,six for the center of mass,one for the elimination of the node,and one for the elimination of time-in all, twelve integrals. The first ten are called the classical Eulerian integrals.Lie's theory on the continuous group of transformations is applied, to reduce the order of the differential equations.The concepts·of contact transformation,involution, function groups,and distinguished functions are introduced for the theoretical discussion of the reduction.The order of the differential equations is reduced to $6 n - 1 2$ by the known classical integrals.The problem is to see whether any further integrals exist,apart from some rigorous particular solutions of the problem treated in Chapter 3.The theorem of Bruns as extended by Painlevé states the nonexistence of new integrals that are algebraic with regard to the momenta.The theorem of Poincaré denies the existence of new integrals that are uniform functions with regard to the Keplerian elements. Then what would be the functional form of the new integrals ? In search for such new integrals we must refer to transcendental functions, or try to solve the equations on the basis of transcendental processes.

The existence of the solution will be proved in a later volume basing it on Weierstrass's theory of analytical functions.The whole field of celestial mechanics at present is occupied by various attempts to solve the equations by an infinite process of iteration,which we treat in several chapters of a future volume. The usual method is to expand the solution in the form of an infinite multiple Fourier series proceeding in powers of the small disturbing masses, with linear functions of time as arguments. Although such a series expansion resembles the one in the theory of quasi-periodic or almost periodic functions,we know little about the condition for which the solution is a quasi-periodic function, so that the series employed in the usual perturbation theory in celestial mechanics cannot be said to represent the solution we are seeking. Can we possibly attack the problem from the function-theoretic standpoint of Poincaré and Painlevé? On the other hand,as will be demonstrated in one of the subsequent volumes,Poincaré has proved that none of the infinite processes ever proposed for solving the problem is uniformly convergent, notwithstanding all the efforts that our predecessors have concentrated on this method of solution.Is it possible to predict the future behavior of the motion on a purely qualitative basis - thus abandoning our original aim of modifying the laws of mechanics through comparing observations with theoretical computations carried out on a nonrigorous mathematical foundation? At this pessimistic stage Poincaré, followed by Birkhoff, founded his whole theory of topology and applied it to the equations of celestial mechanics,in order to study the state of motion in an infinite time interval. These will be the main topics of the future volumes.
