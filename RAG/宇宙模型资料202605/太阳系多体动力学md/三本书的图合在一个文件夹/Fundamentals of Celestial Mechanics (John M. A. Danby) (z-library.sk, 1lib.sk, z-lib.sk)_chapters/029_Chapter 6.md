# Chapter 6

Discussions of the problem of two bodies are found in 11,and many other texts including:

16.Baker,R.M.L. Astrodynamics: Applications and Advanced Topics. New York:Academic Press,1967.   
17.Bate,R.B.,Mueller,D.D.andWhite,J.E.Fundamentals of Astrodynamics. NewYork:Dover Publications,1971.   
18.Battin,R.H. Astronautical Guidance. New York:McGraw Hill, 1964.

19.Brouwer,D.and Clemence,G.M.Methods of Celestial Mechanics. New York: Academic Press,1961. This is the authoritative text on classical celestial mechanics.

20.Escobal,P.R.Methods of Orbit Determination New York:Wiley,1965.

21.Escobal,P.R. Methods of Astrodynamics.New York: Wiley,1968.

22.Fitzpatrick,P.M.Principles of Celestial Mechanics. New York:Academic Press,1970.

23.Gauss, C.F. Theoria Motus. New York: Dover Publications. Originally published in 1809, this makes vivid reading even today,and communicates some of the flavor of Gauss’ genius.

24.Herget,P.The Computation of Orbits.Ann Arbor,MI:Edwards Bros.,Inc., 1948. The outstanding work of its time in this area by the outstanding practitioner in this field. It is by no means outdated.

25.Herrick, S.Astrodynamics,Iand II. New York:Van Nostrand,1971,1972

26.Plummer,H.C. An Introductory Treatise on Dynamical Astronomy. New York:Dover Publications,1960.First published in 1918,this is a classic,and still, for me,one of the best references available.

27.Pollard,H.Mathematical Introduction to Celestial Mechanics. Englewood Cliffs,N.J.:Prentice Hall,1966.Chapters I to III appear in CelestialMechanics,Mathematical Association of America,1976.This text is carefully presented to be mathematically rigorous;it is beautifully written,and very clear.In particular,it is accessible to those with no initial background in mechanics.

28.Roy, A.E. Orbital Motion. Bristol, England: Adam Hilger,Ltd.,1982.

29.Smart,W.M. Celestial Mechanics. Cambridge,England:Cambridge University Press,1953.

30.Taff,L.G.Celestial Mechanics.New York:Wiley,1985.   
31.Watson, J.C.Theoretical Astronomy. Philadelphia:Lippincott,1986.

Fora discussion of the solution of Kepler's equation，with different initial guesses, see:

32.Danby, J.M.A.and Burkardt,T.M.The Solution of Kepler's Equation,I and II.Celestial Mechanics,31, 95-107,317-328,1983；40,303-312, 1987.

K. Stumpff published his paper on universal variables in:

33.Stumpff,K. Neue Formeln and Hilfstafeln zur Ephemeridenrechung,Astr. Nach,275,108-128,1947.

S.Herrick worked independently，and published a paper that will be more accessible to most readers:

34.Herrick, S. Universal Variables. Astron. J. 70,309-315, 1965.

A fundamental paper is

35.Goodyear,W.H. Completely General Closed Form Solution for Coordinates and Partial Derivatives of the Two-Body Problem. Astron. J., 70, 189-192,1965.

Invention in the problem of two bodies is far from dead. For a recent original method see:

36.Shepperd,S.W. Universal Keplerian State Transition Matrix. Celestial Mechanics,35,129-144,1985.

Formulas for the Julian Date, and the solar coordinates are taken from:

37.Almanac for Computers, 1986. Nautical Almanac Offce, U.S. Naval Observatory. Washington: U.S. Government Printing Offce.

More extensive formulas for the Sun, Moon and Planets can be found in:

38.Van Flandern，T.C.and Pulkkinen,K.F. Low-Precision Formulas for Planetary Positions. Ap.J. Supp.,391-411,Nov.,1979.
