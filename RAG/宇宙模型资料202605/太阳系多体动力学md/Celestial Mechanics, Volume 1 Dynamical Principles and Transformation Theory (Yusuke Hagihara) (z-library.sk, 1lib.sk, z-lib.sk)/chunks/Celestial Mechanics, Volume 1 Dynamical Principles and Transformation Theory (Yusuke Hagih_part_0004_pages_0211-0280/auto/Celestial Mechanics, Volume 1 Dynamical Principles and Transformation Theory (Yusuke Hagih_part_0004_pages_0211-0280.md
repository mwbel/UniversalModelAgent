# Particular Solutions of the Many-Body Problem

HOMOGRAPHIC MOTION

# 3.IFUNDAMENTAL EQUATIONS

Before we attempt a general solution to the problem of many bodies we discuss its particular solutions, that is, the rigorous solutions of the problem when the initial motion of the bodies obeys certain conditions. In the case of three bodies Euler discovered a particular solution in which the three bodies always lie on a straight line, their positions being determined by a quintic equation with functions of the three masses as coefficients.This straight line passes through the center of mass of the three bodies,and the bodies individually perform Keplerian motions with the center of mass as one of the foci. Lagrange has found a particular solution in which each of the three bodies lies at a vertex of an equilateral triangle, irrespective of the masses. Each radius vector from the center of mass of the system to each of the masses moves in the same way as the radius vector of a Keplerian motion around the focus.At each instant the three bodies form an equilateral triangle. Lagrange reduced the equations of motion of the three bodies to a system of two differential equations of order 2 and one differential equation of order 3, that is,to a system of differential equations of order 7,and obtained from the system the particular solutions of Euler and of Lagrange. In a special case when each of the bodies performs a circular motion,the particular solution of Euler is called the straight-line equilibrium-point solution and the particular solution of Lagrange the equilateral-triangular equilibrium-point solution. These definitions refer to points in equilibrium, that is,points which are at rest with respect to rotating axes.These solutions are the only particular solutions (apart from the trivial solution in which the three bodies move along a single straight line fixed in space).

When the resultant force acting on each particle in the many-body problem is directed to the center of mass of the system and is of magnitude proportional to the distance of the particle from the center of mass, and when the initial velocity of each particle is in the direction making a fixed angle (the same for all the particles) with the radius vector to the particle drawn from the center of mass and is of magnitude proportional to the magnitude of the radius vector, then, according to Laplace, there exist possible motions in which the radius vectors of the particles at any given instant are mutually in a constant ratio independent of time,and the angular velocities of the particles at any given instant are equal. Such motions are called homographic (Lehmann-Filhés,l89l； Dziobek, 1900; Seydler, l886;Moulton,1900,l904; Pizzetti,1904; Banachiewicz, 1906；Andoyer，1906；Longley，1907；Woronetz，1907；Brehm，1908; Zapp，1908；Bilimovitch，191l；Chazy，1918，1927；Muntz，1922; Lindow,1922,1923,1924,1926,1928；Schaub,1929；MacMilan and Bartkey，1932；Meyer，1933；Williams，1938；Meffroy，1962，1967). Dziobek proved in the case of $_ n$ particles mutually attracted according to Newtonian law the possibility of their homographic motion. Such motions are a generalization of the particular solutions of Euler and Lagrange for the three-body problem.

In the generalized particular solution of Lagrange, the configuration of $\pmb { n }$ particles all in one plane in their homographic motion is called a central figure.The generalized collinear particular solution of Euler,in which all $\pmb { n }$ particles lie on the same straight line at each instant, is also possible. When $n \geq 4$ ， there may occur homographic motions in three dimensions. The motion of each particle is along the corresponding straight line fixed in space,all lines pass through the center of mass; the configuration of the $\pmb { n }$ bodies at each instant remains similar,and the corresponding sides remain parallel and proportional in length. Such a configuration is called homothetic.A homographic motion for which the total angular momentum of the particles is zero forms a homothetic configuration,while a homographic motion for which the total angular momentum is not zero occurs either on a straight line or on a plane and in no other way.As will be shown later,if all particles in the problems of three bodies and of many bodies collide at the same point at the same instant,the angular momentum of the whole system is necessarily zero and the particles tend to one of such homographic configurations at the collisions.We propose to discuss such particular solutions of the $\boldsymbol { n }$ -body problem in the most general manner (Meyer, 1933).
