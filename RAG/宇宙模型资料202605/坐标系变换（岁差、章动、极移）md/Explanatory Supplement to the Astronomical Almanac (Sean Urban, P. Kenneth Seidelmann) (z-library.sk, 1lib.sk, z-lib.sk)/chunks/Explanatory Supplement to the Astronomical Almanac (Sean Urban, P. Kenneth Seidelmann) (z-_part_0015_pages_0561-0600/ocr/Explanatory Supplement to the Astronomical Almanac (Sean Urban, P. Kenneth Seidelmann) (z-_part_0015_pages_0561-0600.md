# 12.4.2 Occultations of Planets

The prediction of occultation of stars (or minor planets)by planets (or by minor planets) largely centers on the search for conjunction in right ascension within the limits of difference of declination that make an occultation possible.These limitsare very small, being approximately the sum of the horizontal parallax and the semidiameter of the planet.

The observations themselves yield useful information on the diameters of planets and about the shape of minor planets.

Theactual prediction follows the basic principles underlying those for eclipses,occultations,and transits；but more direct and less formal methods are used because theangles involved are much smaller and the prediction much less precise.

An occultation willtake place as seen from some point on the Earth's surface,provided the difference $\delta - \delta _ { s }$ in apparent declination at the time of conjunction in right ascension satisfies the condition

$$
\left| \delta - \delta _ { s } \right| < ( \pi + s ) / \left| \sin \rho \right|
$$

where $\pi$ and $s$ arerespectively the equatorial horizontal parallax and semidiameter of the planet and $\rho$ is the position angle of its direction of motion.Note must be taken of the position of the Sun,as for lunar occultation,because it is generally useless to predict occultations of stars in daylight.

Methods of predictionare described by Taylor(1955)；thepaper also described the determination of the actual limits of occultation. The IOTA (see $\ S 1 2 . 4$ provides predictions of planetary occultations (URL[2]) that arealso published in Sky and Telescope.Predictions ofplanetary occultationsarealso published in The Handbookof the British Astronomical Association and the Observer's Handbook of the Royal Astronomical Society of Canada.

# 13.2.2 Double Stars

Double star systems permit the determination of the stellar mass,hence their importance in astrophysics,given that the structure and evolution of a star are basically dependent on two parameters:(1) the initial chemical content,and (2) the initial mass.While most of the main sequence iswell determined,the two endsare still poorlycharacterized,asare stars off the main sequence.Even for stellar typeswhich are well known,additional mass determinations can probe more subtle differences inchemical properties as well as intra-and inter-cluster variations.The numbers and distribution of binary stars also provide insights into the process of star formation.

Doubleand multiple stars constitute an important fraction of the stellar population.The term double star includes optical pairs formed by chance juxtaposition of unrelated stars, aswell as true binaries that are physically related.For many double stars there is not enough information to determine whether the pairis physical; therefore,catalogs also include optical doubles.Historically,double stars havealso been differentiatedby theobservational meansused to discover and study them.Thus,there are not only visual doubles,but also spectroscopic,astrometric,photometric,interferometric,and occultation pairs.With the development of modern observing techniques,however,these distinctions are increasingly blurred.

Multiple systems,containing more than two components,generallyare classedas either hierarchical or nonhierarchical.In the first instance,a typical triple will contain aclose pair andathird component,located at a distance many times the separation of the close pair.These hierarchical multiples,despite beingquite small,are usually virialized.Inthe second instance, the separations of all the components are roughly the same.Examples of these classes are Epsilon Hydrae (hierarchical) and the Trapezium (nonhierarchical).

Nomenclature for double stars is extremely mixed.Visual pairs are usually designated by the discoverer'sabbreviation and serial number,although some observers have neglected tonumberalloraportionof theirpairs.Eclipsingpairsare designatedby the variable star nomenclature.Spectroscopic binaries are usually designated by their Bayer/Flamsteed designation or HD number. Occultation binaries are often denoted by their SAO or ZC number,although thisis byno meansuniversal.There appears to be no standard nomenclature whatsoever for astrometric or other more exotic binaries,which are given a multitude of designations taken from various catalogs.An effort is underway to unify the various nomenclature schemes.For further details on this effort,see the Washington Multiplicity Catalog (WMC) webpage at URL[6].

Withafew exceptions,the primary (A component) of a visual double is the brighter star; the secondary (B component)is fainter.There are systems of very small magnitude difference, however,where this distinction is arbitrary.Further,there are cases where the distinction of which component is brighter depends on the observed wavelength.Additional components in amultiple systemare designated C,D,and so on,in order of discovery;simultaneous discoveries are ordered by increasing separation.It sometimes happens that a new,close, companion is discovered in an already defined system.The conventionis then to designate it with lower case letters.For example,if theAcomponent of a wide pair is split into a close pair,then starAisredesignatedasthe systemAa,Ab.However,these rulesof component designation have not always been historically followed. The WMC is striving to correct all these errors.

Thetableof SelectedDouble Starsin TheAstronomical Almanac givesorbital data for doublestarsinthe tableofBright Stars.Positionsare of the primary starand taken from the Bright Star table.As indicated by the epoch,the orbital data are either the most recent measure(a date inthe past) or calculatedforthe epoch of the edition.The criteria forinclusion in the table is that the pair(1) is listed in the table of Bright Stars;(2) includes the primary; (3)has a separation greater than3arcseconds;and(4) hasamagnitude difference of less than3magnitudes.A few other systems of recent interest are also present.Therefore,the systems that are included are those that would be seen by most observers with a moderatesized telescope.

Also included in The Astronomical Almanac are standards for the Stromgren uvby intermediate-band photometric system and the $\mathrm { H } \beta$ narrow-band system,providing an excellent tool for supplementing the wide-band photometric systems.Wide-band systems give onlyafew parameters that can be used to get information on stellar (and other) characteristics,such as surface temperatures,absolute magnitudes,and interstellar reddening (and absorption).The intermediate and narrow-band photometric indices offer more potential and are designed also to be able to separate parameters.For example,indices are responsive not only to temperature,but toa varietyof otherparameters,such as heavy element abundance and surface gravity.Because they are seen in most stars-they are strongest in stars with a surface temperatures of about10,Ooo kelvin (stellar spectral type A)—the hydrogen Balmer seriesare often used to determine stellar radial velocities by analyzing the Doppler shift of these lines.Photometric standards for these systems are,therefore,importanttoolsin insuring both accurate and precise photometry that can be used for these purposes.

The third setof photometricstandards includedin The Astronomical Almanacare suitable forthereduction of astronomical spectroscopic observations in the optical and ultraviolet wavelengths.Spectroscopy pertains to the dispersion of an object’s light into its component colors (i.e.,energies)and probes the interaction between radiation and matteras a function of wavelength. Spectroscopy ispossible by appropriate techniques inall wavelength regimes; spectrophotometry refers to spectroscopy with visible,near-ultraviolet,and near-infrared light.Applications of spectroscopy are the backbone of astrophysics,probing both the conditions of emissionof radiation and its modification en route to the observer.By dispersing andanalyzinganobject’s light,astronomers can infer thephysical propertiesof that object (such as temperature,mass,luminosity and composition).The standards provide absolute spectral energy distributions with which to calibrate observations using these techniques.

# 13.2.4Radial Velocity Standards

The motion of an object along the line of sight from an observer can be obtained directly from spectroscopic measurements.Radialvelocity observations are used for a wide range of applications (clusterdynamics,galaxy dynamics,stellar pulsations,mass and orbital element determinations in spectroscopic binary systems,etc.),butthe instrumentationused,reduction of data,and standardization of the results are roughly similar.Areliable set of radial velocity standardsare crucial in monitoring instrument stability and zero-points.The dificulty with creating a list of standards has been that they must be non-variable sources in magnitude with a sufficient history of observations.

In the late 198Os,IAU Commission 30on Radial Velocities established a Working Group onStandard Stars,tasked to createasystem of standards.Since this time,the listof standards inTheAstronomical Almanachas been mirroring therecommendationswhich haveresulted from their efforts in establishing a system of standards.The list was initially based on two reports from Commission30 (IAU1957,1973) which combined represented theIAU radial velocity starswith late spectral types at that time.At the IAU General Assemblies in both Kyoto (1997)and Manchester (2Oo0) it was the policy of the reestablished Working Group to remove stars from this provisional list that showed velocity variations greater than about $1 \mathrm { k m / s }$ ,whilecontinuing the work of three observatories developing an officially sanctioned list of standards.Geneva Observatory,Dominion Astrophysical Observatory (DAO),and the Centerfor Astrophysics(CfA) have each been working on establishing the list.The work of the three observatories is summarized ina report ofIAU Commission 30 (IAU1992).

Geneva Observatory has observed potential radial velocity standard stars for over 2O years using the CORAVEL instrument. These include many giants known to possess an intrinsic velocityvariabilityatthelevel ofafewhundredsof $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { s }$ orless.This list is useful for low- and medium-precision work.Using the high-precision velocities from the Elodie spectrometer,it is possible to correct the small systematic color terms in the CORAVEL velocities.A set of 350 dwarf stars is being followed with this instrument with a precision of $1 0 \mathrm { m / s }$ and a zeropoint stability of a few $\mathrm { m } / \mathrm { s }$ ,to form in the future anew set of high-precision standards.Both lists are found at URL[12]; see also Udry et al.(1999).

The results of photographic standard star measurements at DAO have a precision of $1 5 0 \mathrm { m / s }$ overa time span of almost 2O years,with an absolute zero-point linked to asteroids with a precision of $1 0 0 \mathrm { m / s }$ or better.The comparison with Elodie velocities shows anabsence of any color terms.

In the past, the comparison of the Geneva velocities and the combined DAO and CfA velocities showed acolor term,the source ofwhich is still not clear.Work continues on understanding this issue.Until itisresolved,Commission 3O cannot recommendarevised list.There has not been a great amount ofresolution on this issue since the Manchester IAU General Assembly.The emergence of the higher precision radial velocity requirements associated with the study of exoplanets certainly has reinforced the need to continue work to thisend.

Inthe2Oo2 editionof The Astronomical Almanac,a listoffaint stars with radial velocity data (Evans 1968)was added to extend the list to the Southern Hemisphere.There was some trepidation from Commission 3O as to adding these 14 stars,but it was decided they were suitable,since they had been observed for more than 2O years.

The three lists provided by the IAU references were upgraded forthe almanac by adding positions and proper motions from the Hipparcos Catalogue; $V$ magnitudes from the BSC, theHipparcosCatalogue,or SIMBAD;and spectral types from the listofBright Stars (pp.H2-H31),the BSC,the Hipparcos Catalogue,or the original IAU list.

# 13.2.5 Variable Stars

Starswith variable light output are classified based on the type of variability exhibited and aredivided in two maincategories,intrinsic andextrinsic.Theintrinsic variablesare stars undergoing internal physical changes due to pulsations or eruptions. Intrinsic variable stars include pulsating stars (Cepheid variables,RRLyrae stars,RV Tauri Stars,and long period variables such as Mira type stars)and erupting stars (supernovae,novae,recurrent novae,and dwarf novae,symbiotic stars,andRCoronae Borealis stars).Extrinsic variablesare due to external factorsand include stars such as eclipsing binariesand rotating variables.Variable star data can provide a wealth of information about stellar properties that can be used to understand other stars such as mass,radius,luminosity,and temperature.Cepheid variables allowfor determining distances to far away galaxies and the age of the universe.

The variability of magnitude in stars can range from periods of a fraction of a second to years,depending upon the type.Therefore,variable stars need a long history of observations to be correctly classified and understood.The American Association of Variable Star Observers(AAVSO;URL[13]) is an established organizationof professional and amateur astronomers which serves tocoordinate,collect,evaluate,analyze,publish,and archivevariable star observations.

Thelist in The Astronomical Almanac was selected and updated by astronomersat the AAVSO.Itis based on data provided by the AAVsO and,since the 2Ooo edition,information containedwithin the fourth editionof the General CatalogueofVariable Stars(GCVS, Kholopov etal.1996),the Sky Catalogue2000.0,Volume2(Hirshfeld and Sinnott1997), andACatalogueandAtlasofCataclysmicVariables,2ndEdition(Downes,Webbink,and Shara 1997).The selection of these sources is based on having the brightest stars from each classificationrepresented.Specific criteria forinclusion are givenin the Notes and References section of The Astronomical Almanac.Positions and proper motions were updated for the list in TheAstronomical Almanac,starting with the 2006 edition,using theNOMAD database which is consistent with the ICRS (see $\ S \ 1 3 . 1 . 3 . 1$ and URL[3]).

The nomenclature used for variable stars was developed byF.W.A.Argelanderin the mid-1800s.According to this method,the first star discovered inaconstellation is given the letter R,followed by the genitive form of the Latin name of the constellation.Subsequent variables in the same constellation are named S,T,and so on to Z,followed byRR to RZ; then SS to SZ,and so on to ZZ,after which follow AA to AZ,and so on to QZ,the 334th variable in aconstellation.The next variablefound inaconstellation is named V335 plus the genitive. Stars which have been assigned Greek letters priorto the start ofthis system,such as Omicron Ceti,or small Roman letters,such as $^ \mathrm { g }$ Herculis,continue to keep those names.

# 13.2.6 Exoplanets and Host Stars

An extrasolar planet,or exoplanet for short,isa planet that orbits a star other than the Sunand that is beyond the solar system.Most have been detected through radial velocity observations-the Doppler method—and otherindirect methods rather than actual imaging. Given the increased interest and research in exoplanets,adding them to The Astronomical Almanac,starting with the 2O11 version,was considered to be valuable to the observing community.

The table of exoplanets and their host stars is based upon updated versions of the Catalog ofNearby Exoplanets (Butler 2Oo6).The almanac draws from the most currently available version at the time of publicationwhich can be found at URL[14] wherea table of host star characteristics is also available.It contains all the known exoplanets with masses and orbits established by Doppler measurements of stars within 2OO parsecs.Given the number of exoplanets continues to grow rapidly,selection criteria must be applied to keep the table inthe almanac to a manageable size.For example,for the2Ol3 edition of The Astronomical Almanac,a subset from the data set was chosen usinga host star magnitude limit of $V < 8$ Also incorporated into the table are all transiting exoplanets,which pushes the limiting magnitude of the table inthealmanac to about $V = 1 0$ .Thecatalog'sauthor and exoplanet pioneer,P.Butler,suggested that useful propertiesof the exoplanets and host stars from the catalog would fall into two categories.For those most interested in the properties of theplanets,orbital period,eccentricity,and time of periastron are the most helpful.This forinstanceiswhatisneeded to calculate time of transit for the transitingplanets.Those most interested in the host stars of exoplanets would want $B - V$ magnitude,parallax,and metallicity. Positions,proper motionsand parallaxes (where available) for the host stars were taken independently from theNOMAD database(§13.1.3.1; URL[3]).The mid-year positions for the host stars given in The Astronomical Almanac table are improved from those positions listedin the original version of thecatalogas they areboth more accurate and precise;several inconsistencies in the catalog were corrected.

# 13.2.7 Pulsars

Radio pulsarsare rapidly rotating neutron stars having strong magnetic fields.Because of their smallphysical size,pulsars are unresolved by even the largest radio interferometers,and arevaluable as astrometric reference sources.Because of theirlarge moments of inertia,the rotation period of pulsars is extremely regular,in most cases showing only a linear increase in the period with time as the pulsar loses energy.The oldest,coolest pulsars have longtermspin stabilities comparable to the best terrestrial clocksand are being studied foruseas navigational beacons,similar to GPS.The discovery of pulsars twice resulted in the award of theNobel Prize in Physics:in 1974 to Antony Hewish for the discovery of the firstpulsars, and in1993 to Joseph Taylor andRussell Hulse for the discovery of the first binary pulsar, $\mathbf { B } 1 9 1 3 + 1 6$ .

Thelist of pulsars in The Astronomical Almanac dates from the 2oo7 edition.It is derived from Manchester et al. (2oo5; URL[15]) and updated annually.The positions are,in nearly all cases,timing positions which are measures of arrival times on various dates spread over a time period.These are accurate wellunder an arcsecond for“normal”pulsars and under the milliarcsecond level for millisecond pulsars.Positions are in the coordinate system defined by the JPL planetary ephemeris.(The particular ephemeris used may vary from source to source,depending on how recently theanalysis was done.) Ina few cases,interferometric positionswith comparable accuracy have been obtained.

# 13.3 Clusters and Galaxies

# 13.3.1 Open Star Clusters

Open clusters are irregularly shaped groups of stars moving together with roughly the same space motion.They generally liein the galactic plane and contain prominent young stars or star forming regions.Unlike associations which are often dynamically unbound groups of stars less than 1 million years old,open clusters are dynamically bound groups of stars ranging in ages fromless than1million years to more than1billion years.It is theorized that open clusters are those dynamically bound associations which survived being dispersed into smaller groups of stars.Open clusters are distinctly different from globular clusters,which areseveral billion yearsold and found inthe galactic halo.

Open clusters are a crucial element in understanding the structure and history of our Galaxy,given that distances and ages of open clusters can be determined with beter accuracy than forisolated field stars.Opencluster systemsarekey tounderstanding galactic dynamics, because they spanarelativelywide range ofageswhichcan be determinedwith more precision thanany other spiral arm tracer.They are used to better understand the motion of spiral arms to derive the galactic rotation curve.They also provide a means to map the chemical composition in our Galaxy.In addition,open clusters providea set of coeval stars with different masses that canbe used toderiveand test theories of starformation,suchas the initial mass function,rate of matter transferred into stars,the rate of density of cloud,and rate of binary stars.

The table of open clusters was selected by GostaLyngä based upon the 5th(1987) edition of the Lund-Strasbourg catalog(original edition described by G.Lynga 1981).The clusters were selected on the basis of the availability of dataat the timeand,therefore,comprise the most observed open clusters.The selection of the clustersremained while the data were updated over the years.Forthe2Oo5 edition ofThe AstronomicalAlmanac,cluster data were updated using theNew Catalog ofOptically Visible Open Clustersand Candidates (Dias et al.2002).The online version of thecatalog isavailable at URL[16],where additional data parameters are given and updated annually.The Astronomical Almanac for 2O13 uses version 3.1of this catalog.Another useful resource for open cluster datais John-Claude Mermiliod's WEBDAopenclusterdatabaseatURL[17].

Listed in the table of open clusters are a number of associations that might not develop into physically bound clusters.These are the Trapezium cluster and some of the Collinder objects. TheBlancoand Upgrenclustersare considered physical entities,because their members have similarmotionsand physicalproperties,although theyare onlyasmallpartof the total stellar population inside the volume they occupy. Clusters that are only the apparent result of the distribution of obscuring clouds have been excluded from the list.

Two identifications are given for each cluster.The first is the designation adopted by theIAU,while the secondis the traditional name (Alter etal.197O).The International Astronomical Union decided in 1979 ona nomenclature consisting of a“C" for cluster,four digits for hours and minutes of right ascension (195o.O) and a plus or minus sign plus three digits for degrees and tenths of degrees of declination (1950.0).

All data parameters for the open clusters are taken from the New Catalogof Optically Visible Open Clusters and Candidates (Dias et al. 2oo2) including positions and proper motions.Positions are consistent with the ICRS and referred to the mean equator and equinox of the middle of the Julian year.

The tabulated angular diameter of a cluster refers to the cluster's nucleus.The published values of angular diameters of clusters sometimes differ bya large factor due to different limiting magnitudes for different studies and to different opinions about the extent of each cluster.

Distances refer to the open cluster's distance from the Sun.Its determination depends on the chemical composition which is not known for many clusters.Assuming a solar composition,asis usually done,introduces some uncertainty.The most reliable way to determine the distance of an open cluster is to observe its color-magnitude diagram and fit the diagram to the main sequence of nearby stars.When a cluster includes a number of main sequence stars,this method of determining distances is superior to other methods used for distant stars or star systems.For the distance of anearby cluster with a long main sequence, a precision as low as $10 \%$ can be achieved.

# 13.3.2 Globular Star Clusters

Formore than acentury,globularclustershave provided astronomers with testbed laboratories forall kinds of astrophysical processes.First noticed for their variable star populations (RR Lyraestars,Population IICepheids,red-giant variables,novae),they laterbecame essential proving grounds for the development of the theory of stellar structure and stellar evolution, andthey remain the single most important way to measure the age of our Galaxy.Globular clustersarevery tightly bound,giving themspherical shapesand relatively high stellar densities toward their centers,and contain more stars than the less dense open clusters.Found inthe halo ofa galaxy,globular clustersare fairly common.Itis thought that globular clusters contain some of the oldestand first stars to be produced in agalaxy and formed with the parent galaxyrather than as distinctly separate objects.The 197Os and198Ossaw the discoveries of newhigh-energy sourceswithin globular clusters (X-ray binaries and millisecond pulsars), and computer modeling of the internal dynamical evolution of clusters allthe way through to core collapse.Newer observations are concentrating on tracing the faintest stars within the clusters (the bottom ends of the ancient white-dwarf sequence),and on the characteristics of globular clusters in other galaxies.

Thelistof globular clustersin The Astronomical Almanac isprovidedbyWilliamE. Harris,who maintains a database ofMilky Way globular clusters at URL[18].The online list isupdated regularlyand provides dataon distances,velocities,metallicities,luminosities, colors,and dynamical parameters.

# 13.3.4Quasi-Stellar Objects

A quasar (the name is actually a contraction of “quasi-stellar radio source")is an extremely bright and distant active galactic nucleus.There was a great deal of initial controversy over the nature of quasars,but there is now a general consensus that they are a compact halo of mattersurroundinga supermassive black hole.They are observed as high redshift sources in the infarared,optical,ultraviolet,radio,X-Ray and gamma ray frequencies.They seem ratherstellar (point-like),rather than extended sources similar to galaxies.They display rapid changes in luminosity and are believed to be powered by the accretion of material into their nuclei,making these luminous versions of active galaxies.No other currently known mechanism appears able to explain the vast energy output and rapid variability.

At present,more than 1Oo,ooo quasars are known，all with redshifts ranging from $0 . 0 6 > \mathbf { z } > 6 . 4$ ；redshift isdefinedas $\mathrm { z } = \left( \lambda - \lambda _ { 0 } \right) / \lambda _ { 0 }$ ,where $\lambda _ { 0 }$ is the laboratory wavelength for the spectral line being observed.Quasar redshiftsare almost always measured with the broad-emission lines of hydrogen,helium,and other heavy elements that are characteristic of quasars.Most quasars are brightest in their rest-frame near-ultraviolet (near the $1 2 1 6 { \mathring \mathrm { A } }$ (Lyman-alpha line of hydrogen),but due to the tremendous redshifts of these sources, that peak luminosity hasbeen observed as far as $9 0 0 0 \mathring { \mathrm { A } }$ in the near infrared.The redshifts correspond to distances between 78O million and 28 billion light-years.Due to these great distances,quasars offer a glimpse into the very early universe.At this time,the Sloan Digital Sky Survey is in the process of measuring quasar properties; once complete,it will provide redshift values for approximately 10o,ooo quasars.

Thelistof quasars is originally fromA Catalogue of Quasars and Active Galactic Nuclei, lst ed.(Veron-Cetty and Veron 1984) using selection parameters suggested by Tim Heckman. These selection parameters did not have any physical significance other than allowing a list ofquasars with a variety ofcharacteristics.Since this catalog has been updated many times, TheAstronomicalAlmanac for 2Oo9 used the12th edition of thecatalog(Veron-Cetty and Veron 20o6;URL[21]).When updated,the aim for the list was to be a subsample of those types that are popular in current research interests:the optically brightest,strongest in radio and X-ray,highest known redshifts,gravitational lenses,brightest quasars with interesting absorptionsystems (e.g.,PHL 52oo),high redshift quasars suitable forHe IIGunn-Peterson observations (e.g., $\mathrm { H } 1 7 0 0 { + } 6 4$ ,QSO 0302-003).

# 13.4.2 Radio-FluxCalibrators

Calibration isa necessary step in the reduction of radio flux observations for research purposes.Absolute calibration of radio flux data is required to delete systematic effects such as those from the receiver,the antenna,or the atmosphere.An accurate calibration ofthe observations enables comparison of data observed at different telescopes and at different times.All fields of radio astronomy require accurate absolute calibration,especially variability studies of radio sources.Calibration of measurements also allows the means to determine the telescope efficiency,offering the possibility to study the properties (e.g.,surface RMS) of the antenna.

Using sources,like the ones given in The Astronomical Almanac,are the easiest way to calibratearadio telescope.A direct,absolute calibration is very difficult especially for large telescopes (if possible at all).The three primary standards-the strongest three radio sources (CasA,CygA,and Tau A)—are unsuitable for the calibration of large antennas.Absolute calibration of these three primary standards requires the use of small antennas given these sources,particularly at high frequencies,have finite sizesandareall partially resolved using largeantennas.It is necessary to havea list of secondary calibrators,the flux densities of which are determined with reference to these primary standards.Many,if not all,centimeter wavelength radio telescopes use the secondary flux density standards provided in this table.

The data in the table were originally taken from Baars et al.(1977)and updated nearly everyyearby Kraus,Krichbaum,Pauliny-Toth andWitzel with new measurements.The original data are derived as follows:

1.The absolute spectrum of Cas A,with its secular rate of decrease taken into account, was derived for epoch198o.0 between frequenciesof $3 0 0 \mathrm { M H z }$ and $3 0 \mathrm { G H z }$ .   
2.The absolute spectra of Cyg Aand Tau Awere derived from direct measurements and from measurements of the ratios of their flux density to that of CasA.   
3.Anaccurate spectrum for the somewhat weaker source Vir A was established from direct,accurate ratios of its flux density to those of Cas A and CygA.   
4.This VirA spectrum was used as a basis for obtaining accurate relative spectra of the secondary sources provided in the table.

Forflux calibration of interferometers and synthesis telescopes,only three sources are suitable:3C48,3C147,and 3C286.Positions for 3C48 and 3C147are taken from Elsmore and Ryle(1976).The position of 3C 286 is taken from the list of astrometric radio sources. Other sources may need acorrection for angular size when used with the largest single antennas at high frequencies.

There are two important notes of caution in using these standards.Extrapolation of flux densities beyond the given frequency range printed is not advisable.Also,some sources contain compact,opaque components that may be variable.For example,3C48 showssome variability around $1 . 6 \mathrm { G H z }$ and $2 0 \mathrm { G H z }$ (PerleyandCrane 1985).The flux density of 3C147is variable (Simonetal.1983;Andrew,MacLeod,andFeldman1981) asis its structure (Preuss etal.1984).Foraccurate calibrations offlux density measurements,several sources from the tableshouldbeutilizedinordertoaccountforsucheffects.

A table with polarization data for the most prominent sources is also provided by A. Kraus which gives the polarization degree and angle for a number of frequencies.

# 13.4.3 X-Ray and Gamma Ray Sources

In the broadest terms,an X-ray or gamma ray source can be defined as any source emitting X-rays or gamma rays.Since most $\mathrm { _ X }$ -rays and gamma rays are emitted when anabrupt change occurs in a charged particle's trajectory near the source,sources are required to have a strong gravitational/magnetic field.The types of X-ray and gamma ray sources have increased over the years as instruments became better able to detect such sources.It has long been known that our Sun is both an $\mathrm { \Delta X }$ -ray and gamma ray source (many X-ray sources turn out to be gamma ray sources,but not all),and ithasalso been known forawhile that black holesand neutronstars (both isolated onesand thosein binary systems,whetherapulsar or not) are both X-ray and gamma ray sources. Other sources are active galactic nuclei(AGNs),quasars,many galaxies,and supernovae.More recently,it has been discovered thatthe intergalactic medium in some parts of the sky emits copious amounts of X-rays.Gamma ray bursts (GRBs)are the most energeticof gammaray sources,but they have also been found to produce X-raysafter the initial burst of gamma rays.Even the source of so-called “cosmic rays”has been recently discovered to be many very faint X-ray and gamma ray sources,that mix theiremissions with those from more easily identified sources to produce the cosmic ray background that has been known formanyyears.

To detect X-ray sources,instruments must be placed outside the Earth's atmosphere, since it does a good job of blocking most X-rays from geting to the Earth'ssurface.Most gammarays are blocked by the atmosphere so many gammaray instruments are put into orbit also.However,the most energetic gamma rays (those in theGeV-TeVenergy range) can get through the Earth'satmosphere and so there are some gamma ray instruments on the ground that seek to detect these very energetic particles.

These sources are of interest to optical astronomers for multiple reasons.It is of interest to know,for example in the case ofX-ray binaries,what kindof star is supplying the matter that feeds the black hole or neutron star.By knowing its magnitude (whether visual or infrared) an educated guess can be made about the size and age of the companion star.This is useful for determining the orbit of the X-ray or gamma ray source and its mass and age.Itis also interesting to know how the X-ray/gamma ray source affects itscompanion star.The loss of mass from the companion to the X-ray/gammaray source can change how the star evolves. In some cases,it has been speculated that the known X-ray binary was initially two non X-Ray emitting starsina binary system,and their interaction caused a supernova that gave birth to theX-ray source that we now detect.In the case of isolated pulsars,many of which arefirstobserved at radio wavelengths,it is interesting to know if thesepulsars emit energy at other wavelengths.Manydo;the Crab pulsarand the Vela pulsar being two well-known examples.Even more recently,once GRBs were shown to have an X-ray afterglow,further optical observations revealed an optical afterglow as well.And of course,since the source of GRBs was (and still is) unknown,these optical observations often showed a galaxy at or near the supposed location of the GRB.The current understanding of GRB sources is that they occur in galaxies and might be even more energetic than Supernovae (one theory calls these sources“Hypernovae").Optical observations of galaxies,AGN,and Quasarscanagain give some insight to these X-ray/gamma ray sources by determining theirage,what kind of stars they are composed of,and other posible mechanisms that might help the high-energy astronomer understand the X-ray/Gamma ray emission process from these sources.

TheX-Ray and gammaraysources listed in The Astronomical Almanac arewel-studied andhistorically important sources drawn from published results,selected byindividualsin this particular field of research.The X-Ray binary sources are from the catalogs of van Paradijs (1995)and Liu,van Paradijs and van den Heuvel (2Ooo,20o1).Other sources are selected from the Fourth Uhuru Catalog (Forman etal.1978).Positions for X-ray sources are taken from NOMAD as described in $\ S \ 1 3 . 1 . 3 . 1$ (URL[3]).The gamma ray sources are selected from three catalogs to cover their broad energy range:low-energy gamma rays (photon energies $< 1 0 0 \ \mathrm { k e V }$ ）aretakenfromTheFourthIBIS/ISGRISoftGamma-Ray Survey Catalog (Bird etal.2O1O); high-energy gamma rays (photon energies between $1 0 0 \mathrm { M e V }$ and $1 0 0 \mathrm { G e V } )$ aretakenfrom FermiLargeArea TelescopeSecond SourceCatalog (Fermi-LATCollaboration 2O11);and very-high-energy gamma rays (photon energies above $1 0 0 \mathrm { G e V }$ )are taken from TeVCatOnlineCatalogforTeVAstronomy availableatURL[24]. Some sources are bright in two or all three energy ranges.

# 14.1 Introduction to Computing Techniques

In this section we will examine several computing techniques of special relevance to astronomical calculation.For a more general treatment,see textbooks on computing methods and numericalanalysis.A short note onelementary computing principles isalsoincludedin Interpolation and Allied Tables (H.M.Nautical Almanac Office 1956),which covers the sources of mistakes,the nature of checks,and the nature and effect of the inevitable errors due to rounding-off and other causes.These topics,although of fundamental importance,are not discussed here.

An important principle of computation is that the data used determine the maximum precision of a calculation.This precision can be reduced by poor computing—for example, byapoor choice of formula or by the failure to retain an adequate number of figures in the intermediate stages-but it can never be increased.Generally no simple relation exists between the errors (absolute or relative)of the final result andof the data,though arelation can readily be seen numerically by following through the calculation step by step,and often geometrically.It is misleading to quotearesult to more figures than is justifiedby the data on which it is based,and it is erroneous todo so ifan inadequate number of figures have been retained in the intermediate stages.

Retaining more figures than necessary is a waste of effort if it significantly increases the work involved.In some operations,however, especially in those done on a computer, extra figures involve very litleadditional work;but in other operations,anextra figure may require amore elaborate formula or extra word length,resulting in much more additional work.A suitable number of figures is that which offers the greatest convenience consistent with the building-up error,due to accumulation of rounding-offs,not exceeding the errorof the data; thismust be judged in relation to each calculation.

Mathematical formulae,if correct,adequately give a desired precision in the result;but they may be inefficient, inconvenient,and misleading.One example is the inefficiency of determining a small angle from its cosine;for a stated precision of angular measurement the number of decimals in the cosineis inversely proportional to the angle;and forafixed number of decimals in the cosine (the usual and convenient case)the precision obtainable decreases asthe angle decreases.Consider,for example,the formula for the third side of a spherical triangle,(Fig.14.1) given two sides and the included angle,

# 14.2.3 Inverse Interpolation

Inverse interpolation to derive the interpolating factor $p$ ,and hence the time,forwhich the function takes a specified value $f _ { p }$ is carried out by successive approximations. The first estimate $p _ { 1 }$ is obtained from

$$
p _ { 1 } = ( f _ { p } - f _ { 0 } ) / \delta _ { 1 / 2 } .
$$

This value of $P$ is used to obtain an estimate of $B _ { 2 }$ ,from the critical table or otherwise,and hence an improved estimate of $p$ from

$$
\begin{array} { r } { p = p _ { 1 } - B _ { 2 } ( \delta _ { 0 } ^ { 2 } + \delta _ { 1 } ^ { 2 } ) / \delta _ { 1 / 2 } . } \end{array}
$$

This last step is repeated until there is no further change in $B _ { 2 }$ or $\boldsymbol { p }$ ;the effects of higher-order differences may be taken into account in this step.
