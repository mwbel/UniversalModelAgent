# 5.3.5.3Atmospheric Loading

The procedure described below is taken from the IERS Conventions (2O03) (IERS 2004).A time-varying atmospheric pressure distribution can induce crustal deformation.For example, pressure variations on the orderof2O hectoPascal (hPa)—and even larger—at mid-latitudes are observed in synoptic pressure systems with length scales for $1 0 0 0 { - } 2 0 0 0 \ \mathrm { k m }$ and periods of approximately two weeks.Seasonal pressure changes due to air mass movements between the continents and oceans can have amplitudes of up to $1 0 \ \mathrm { h P a }$ in particular over the large land masses of the northern hemisphere.At the interannual periods,basin-wide air pressure signals with amplitudes of several hPa also contribute to the spectrum of the loading signal.

Other surface loads due to changes in snow and ice cover,soil moisture and groundwater, as well as ocean-bottom pressure also contribute to surface displacements.For example,at seasonal time scales,itisexpected that the contributionof hydrological loads to surface displacements exceeds the one from air pressure (Blewitt et al.2Oo1).However,while the atmospheric load is fairly wellknown from global air pressure data sets,no suficient models for ocean bottom pressure,snow and soil moisture exists at this time.Therefore,in the following,focusison atmospheric loading although the discussion appliesto any other surface loading.

Theoretical studies by Rabbel and Zschau (1985),Rabbel and Schuh (1986),vanDam and Wahr (1987),and Manabe et al.(1991) demonstrate that vertical crustal displacements of up to $2 5 \mathrm { m m }$ are possible at mid-latitude stations due to synoptic pressure systems.Annual signals in the vertical are on the order of $1 { - } 2 \mathrm { m m }$ ,butmaximum signals of more than $3 \mathrm { m m }$ arepossible over largepartsofAsia,Antarctica,Australia and Greenland (Mangaroti et al.2Oo1；Dong et al.2Oo2).Pressure loading effects are larger at higherlatitude sites due to the more intensive weather systems (larger in amplitude and more spatially coherent).Effects are smaller at mid-latitude sites and at locations within $5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ of the sea or ocean due to the inverted barometer response of the ocean.In all cases,horizontal crustal deformationsare about one-third the amplitude of the vertical effects.

Two basic methods for computing atmospheric loading corrections to geodetic data have beenapplied so far:1） using geophysical models or simple approximations derived from thesemodels;and2)using empirical models based on site-dependent data.The geophysical model approach is analogous to the methods described to compute ocean tidal loading effects. Both methods suffer from a number of disadvantages (see IERS 2004, $\ S \ 7 . 1 . 6 )$ .In2002 the IERS recommended thatcorrections for,among other surface loadings,atmospheric loading should be determined using the geophysical model approach and obtained from the Special Bureau on Loading (SBL) at URL[22].

Regression coefficients based ona geophysical model are already available for anumber ofVLBI sites through the SBL web page and the IERS Conventions’web page,URL[23]. The regresson coefficients were computed using 18 years of the National Centers for EnvironmentalPrediction(NCEP)ReanalysisData(1Jan.1980 to 31Dec.1997).The data are 6-hour values of surface pressure given on a $2 . 5 ^ { \circ } \times 2 . 5 ^ { \circ }$ global grid.Vertical crustal motions at a particular site are modeled by convolving Farrell's (1972) Green's functions for aGutenberg-BullenA Earth model. The regression results $\left( { \mathrm { m m } } / { \mathrm { m b a r } } \right)$ are determined via a linear regresson between the modeled crustal displacements and the local surface pressure determined from the NCEP data set.An inverted barometer model was used in determining the ocean's response to pressure.

# 5.3.6 Plate Motions

One of the factors that affect the definition of a terrestrial reference frame is the motion of the tectonic plates that make up the Earth's surface.As the plates move,fixed coordinates for the observing stationswillbecome inconsistent witheach other.The rates of relative motions forsome regular observing sites are believed to be approximately 5centimeters per year or larger(derived from the dating of rocksand other geophysical information).Plate motions can be measured to a precision of a fewmillimeters per year or better using space geodetic techniques suchasVLBI, SLR,and GPS.There isconsiderableagreement between motions measured overa few years by space geodesy andthe predictions of global plate motion models that average over the pastfew million years.

# 5.3.7Tidal Effects in UT1

To properly compare UT1results obtainedatdifferent epochs,the high-frequency tidaleffects mustbeaccounted for. Thus,a standard expression for the tidal variations in UT1is required. The periodic variations in UT1 due to tidal deformation of the polar moment of inertia have beenrederived(Yoder,Williams,andParke1981)including the tidal deformationofthe Earth with a decoupled core.

This model leads to effective Love numbers that differ from the bulk value of 0.301 because of the oceansand the fluid core,which give rise to different theoretical values of the ratio $k / C$ forthe fortnightly and monthly terms.However, Yoder etal.recommend the value of 0.94 for $k / C$ forboth cases.Periodic terms in UT1are given in Table5.9and Table5.10 andare discussed in IERS Explanatory Supplement (IERS 2OO9)and Gross (20O7).Table 5.9 includes terms due to zonal tides,with periodsup to 35 days,with $k / C = 0 . 9 4$ Table5.10 lists the remainder of the tidal terms (with periods greater than 35 days).

Oceanic tides with short periods (diurnal and sub-diurnal) cause variations in UTl and polar motion that are only partially representedby the model given below.According to Brosche etal.(1989) and Seiler and Wuinsch (1995),the contribution of the oceanic tides should be split into a part that is in phase with the solid Earth tidesand an out-of-phase part.The discrepancy between themodel of Yoder etal.(which alreadycontains the general influence of the oceans)and the model of Brosche et al.,amounts to a maximum of $\pm 0 . 0 8$ msec for thefortnightly and monthly terms.The influence of the ocean tides with periods less than or equal to $2 4 ^ { \mathrm { h } }$ is at the level of $\pm 0 . 1$ msec.Kantha,Stewart,and Desai (1998) found that both $M _ { f }$ and $M _ { m }$ tides have amplitudes of $\sim 0 . 1 1 5$ msec.Thus,the impact of these tides on length of day changes is significant.

Following the IERSConventions(20l0) (IERS201O),it is recommended thata combination of the Yoder et al.(1981) elastic body tide,Wahr and Bergen (1986)anelastic body tide,and Kantha,Stewart,and Desai (1998)ocean tide models are to be used to model longperiod ocean tide effects on the rotation of the Earth.The Kantha,Stewart,andDesai (1998) ocean tide model replaces the Defraigne and Smits (1999) model recommended in the IERS Conventions (2Oo3) (IERS 2O04).That model leavesa large signal at the fortnightly tidal period when removed from length of day observations (Gross 2009).

Before describing the observational systemsand services,the basic sensitivity of the systems should be briefly explained.All the observational systems depend on observing targets whose position can be inferred in some celestial reference frame.This includes stars and solar system objects for optical astrometry,the Moon and artificial satellites for laser ranging,andradio sourcesfor radio interferometry.Theobservationsof these targetsare then used to infer the motion of the observing instruments or,more specifically,the Earth to which they are attached.After the predicted variations fromall other“defined’sources (as explained previously,including precession,nutation,the Earth's sidereal rotation,tides,etc.) havebeen removed,themotion that remains isattributed to polar motionand UT1-UTC variations.Systems using artificial Earth satellites depend on the sensing of alocal “inertial" coordinate system,rather than an astronomically defined one.

It is also important to note that a single instrument (or single baseline for interferometric observing)can be used to determine only two components of the Earth'sorientation.The components commonly determined using single-station (or single-baseline） data include variationinlatitude $( \Delta \phi )$ due to any north-south motion of the station,and UTO-UTC when there isany east-west motion of the station,atributable to Earth orientation variations. To obtain all three components of Earth orientation,it is necessary to use data from more sites.

The equations for expressing $\Delta \phi$ and UTO-UTC in terms of $x _ { p }$ , $y _ { p }$ ,and UT1-UTC are

$$
\Delta \phi = x _ { p } \cos \lambda - y _ { p } \sin \lambda
$$

$$
\mathrm { U T 0 - U T C } = \tan \phi ( x _ { p } \sin \lambda + y _ { p } \cos \lambda ) + ( \mathrm { U T 1 \mathrm { - } U T C } )
$$

where $\phi$ and $\lambda$ are the astronomicallatitude and longitude of the station,with longitude again measured toward the east.

# 5.4.1Very Long Baseline Interferometry

In geodetic Very Long Baseline Interferometry (VLBI),the objects being observed area set of extragalactic radio sources.These objects,quasars and Active Galactic Nuclei (AGN), areso distant that their space motionsare undetectable by current means,so theoretically they forma fixed celestial reference frame.See $\ S 4 . 2$ of this book for further discussion on the International Celestial Reference Frame (ICRF).Currently,more than 34OO radio source positions make up the ICRF.

The VLBIobserving networks consist of two or more radio telescopes that simultaneously observe extragalactic radio sources.More than two radio telescopes are required todetermine all Earth orientation parameters.Each telescope observes over several frequency bands, typicallyseparatedbyafactorof4to10 in frequency,²toallowestimationof theionospheric delay (Ma 2Oo9). The signals are recorded in digital form on large data capacity discs, alongwith precise time signals provided by hydrogen-maser frequency standardsand by incorporating GPS receivers for comparison of the local time scale with a global time scale. Typically,6O quasars are tracked by eight observing stations several times over thecourse of a24-hour observing session.Additionally,two to three stations make daily observations for 1-2 hours("Intensive”sessions) for UT1 determination. Overlapping networks and network sessions ofup to 2O stations connect the 4O global VLBI stations.The data are shipped or electronically transferred toacorrelatorcenter.The differences inarrival timesof theradio source signals (delays),and their change with respect to time (delay rates) are determined between each pair of stations.Similar to the laser ranging solutions,these delays,with better than $1 \mathrm { { c m } }$ precision,and delay ratesare then used in a least-squaresadjustment in order to estimateavariety of geophysical parameters and the coordinates of the radio sources.

Currently 40 radio telescopes in the world have been outfited for making high-accuracy geodetic VLBI observations (using Mark IV/VorK5 equipment,see Schluiter and Behrend, 2007).Although many of these radio telescopes are shared between geodetic and astrophysical observations,one third are dedicated primarily to determination of Earth Orientation Parameters (EOP). Current accuracies of the data obtained include polarmotion at $5 0 { - } 8 0 \mu$ as, UT1-UTCat $6 { - } 7 \mu \mathrm { s }$ from $2 4 ^ { \mathrm { h } }$ sessions and at $1 0 { - } 1 5 \mu \mathrm { s }$ from $1 ^ { \mathrm { h } }$ sessions, nutation and precession(celestial pole) corrections at $5 0 \mu \mathrm { a s }$ ,radio source coordinatesat O.25mas,and VLBI station coordinates ata fewmillimeters (Schliter and Behrend 2007).

TheInternational VLBI Service(IVS)for Geodesy and Astrometry (Schluiter and Behrend 2007),formerly the International VLBI Service,coordinates the acquisition and reduction of VLBI products.Information is available at the IVS website at URL[24].

# 5.4.2Global Navigation Satelite System

Thereare four primary Global Navigation Satelite System(GNSS) systemsin existence. Each of these systems,GPS,GLONASS,and the planned GALILEO and Compass,willbe described. The International GNSS Service (IGS),formerly the International GPS (Global Positioning System) Service,operates a global network of more than 35O permanent,continuously operating GPS and GLONASS tracking sites.Routine operations began on January1, 1994.The following details the current status (as of 2O12) of these as well as the GALILEO navigational satellite systems.The IGS tracking network is available at URL[25]forreal-time information on IGS stations.Further information about these and other navigational satellite systems can be found in Hofmann-Wellenhof,Lichtenegger,and Wasle (2008).

The Global Positioning System was designed as anall-weather,continuous,and global satellite navigationsystem.It was designed to be used fornavigation,but it can also be used for time transfer and geodetic positioning of points on the ground.It was designed and developed by the U.S.Department of Defense (DoD). Once operated solely by the U.S.Department of

Defense,GPS is now operated jointly by acommitee of civilian and DoD agencies (Joint Program Office).

The“space segment’of the system consists of 24 satellites (and spares) in orbits with aperiod of 11 hours58 minutes and an altitude of $2 0 { , } 2 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ .Thereisarequirementof 24satelites for operational status;however,asmany as29-3O satelites broadcast usable tracking signals.They are equally spaced in six planes,inclined at $5 5 ^ { \circ }$ to the equator. Currently,approximately 3O satellites are operational.Each satellite broadcasts on two $L$ - band frequencies, $L _ { 1 }$ $1 5 7 5 . 4 2 ~ \mathrm { M H z } )$ and $L _ { 2 }$ $( 1 2 2 7 . 6 0 ~ \mathrm { M H z } )$ )，with the two frequencies allowing the elimination of first-order ionospheric refraction effects.Modulated onto these frequencies are three codes,including:(1)“the navigation message",containing information (at50 bits/second) on the position of the satelite and information about the satellite itself (such as diagnostics);(2) a coarse acquisition “C/A”code,apseudorandom noise (PRN) code at a frequency of $1 . 0 2 2 \mathrm { M H z }$ ,which repeats every one millisecond (as of this writing, thelimitation on the number of PRN codes restricts the number of broadcasting satellites to 32);and(3) a precise“P”code,another PRN code ata frequency of $1 0 . 2 3 \ : \mathrm { M H z }$ ，which repeats every 38 weeks,but is reset in practice every week.Each satelite also carriesacesium or rubidium clock to provide precise time and stable frequency information.

GPSreceivers“lock on”to multiple (upto 12) satellite signals,generating C/A-(or for more precision,P-） codes,shifting those codes in time to match those received from the satellites,and decoding the D-code information from the satellites.A Standard Positioning Service is available to all users.As one of the optionsavailable,this service allowsuse oftheC/A code (priorto May2,20oo,there wasa degraded signal for the BlockⅡI satelites—ina process knownas Selective Availability (SA)),for determining the receiver's position anywhere on Earth to approximately 10O-2O0 meters.Access to theP-code (Precise Positioning Service) requires more specialized receivers but makes positional accuracies of $1 5 { - } 2 0 \mathrm { m }$ possible.Once“locked on”to the satellites,real-timeupdatesareavailableas often as every 30 seconds.

Differential techniques are a second option which allows near real-time cm-level relative positioning. In these techniques permanent ground stations continuously track GPS satellites and supply their observations over acommunications link to mobileusers.The observations may then be analyzed to get very high-accuracy positions for the mobile receivers.Since any change in the satelite signals willaffect both receivers approximately equally (depending on the distance separating the two satellites),any degradation or coding of the signals will have little effect on the accuracy of the relative station positions.Inorder to increase the positional accuracy,observationscan simply be made forlonger periods,and with more satellites.

A third option is to use the $L _ { 1 }$ and $L _ { 2 }$ carrierwave signalsina differential or inference mode that is analogous to the VLBI technique.In this case,the relative positions of two sites canbe establishedat the fewmm-level on baselines ofup toa few hundred kmin length. Centimeter-level accuracy isavailable on baselines of much longerlength,butuncertainties introduced by the Earth'satmosphere and the satelite positions cause increasing errors with increasing baseline length.Ashasalready beendescribed inChapter3,inaddition to the positional information,the user’s clock can be set relative to the GPS satellite clocks to accuracies as good as $\pm 0 . 1$ nanosecondsorbetter,thusprovidinga time-transfermechanism of extremely high accuracy.

For the purposes of Earth orientation,at least six satellite-tracking stationsare in use at known locations.Carrier-phase measurements are used.It is then possible to determine the position of the satellites,aswell as the orientation of the Earth with respect to the inertial coordinate system realized by the satellites.Operationally,such tracking isadministered by IGS,the Control Segment, (Department ofDefense tracking stations responsible for operating the satelite),and other government networks or private groups.Since July17,1995,when GPS became fully operational (Steigenberger et al. 2OO6),high-accuracy EOP values have been computed and distributed on a regular basis.

Asystem similar to GPS,knownas the Global Navigation Satelite System(GLONASS) ofRussia,willhave seven satellites in each of the three orbital planes (and three spares)for a total of21-24 satellites at full operational status.The satellites are at altitudes of $1 9 1 0 0 \mathrm { - k m }$ in circular orbitswith periods of11 hours15 minutes located in three orbital planes inclined at $6 4 . 8 ^ { \circ }$ totheequator.Each GLONASSsatelliteutilizesadifferent frequency to broadcast its signal.

The European Space Agency (ESA) GALILEO navigational system launched its first experimental satellite on December 28,2Oo5.GALILEO is expected to be partially operational by 2013 and willpossibly reach full operational status by 2O16.The first two satellites of the GALILEO constellation were launched in October 2011.Two more satellites are expected to be launched in 2O12.Thirty satelliteswill have altitudes of $2 3 2 2 2 \mathrm { - k m }$ in orbits with periods of14hours 22 minuteslocated in three orbital planes inclined at $5 6 ^ { \circ }$ to the equator. Like GPS,each GALILEO satellitewill broadcast at multiple frequencies,but all satellites will use the same set of frequencies.

The first satellite of the Compass navigation satelite system (CNSS）was launched onApril14,2Oo7.The Beidou or Compass-M1 systemwill share characteristics of frequency,signal structure,and services with GPS and GALILEO(Gao et al.2Oo8).Compass will be comprised of 30 medium Earth orbit (MEO) satellites and5 geostationary(GEO) satellites.

Currently,GPS provides daily polar motion accuracies of O.1 mas or better. Additionally, dailylength of day accuracies are presently 0.O2-O.03 ms/day (Table5.12).Unlike VLBI, GNSS techniques cannot determine UT1 nor precession-nutation (Gross 2007). However, a UT1-like quantitycalled UTGPScan be used for extrapolating UT1-UTC past the latest VLBI measurement by GPSorbit modeling (Kammeyer 2Ooo; Tracey, Kammeyer,and Stamatakos 2008).Information regarding GNSS and associated products is available at the IGS website at URL[25].

# 5.4.3 Laser Ranging

Laserranging tocelestial targets was first proposed for theMoon in the late 195Os and early 1960s (Alley1983).A laserbeam is directed ataretroreflector on theMoon or artificial satelite.Half the round-trip travel timeof the light multiplied by the velocityof light gives the distance to the object (subject to various corrections due to relative motion,refraction, etc.).These ranges can then be comparedto their predicted values,and in subsequent analyses, corrections to the parameters involved,such as the orbital elements,thestation positions,and the orientation of theEarth,maybe estimated.

The most significant contribution of laser ranging today is its contribution to the development of the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF).Laser ranging is the only technique that can determine the originof ITRFand,along with VLBI,its scale.Since the new millennium,laser ranging is also monitoring the minute offsets of the origin of the ITRF fromthe“instantaneous”center of mass of Earth; itsaccuracy is at the millimeterlevel(Pavlis 2002).

Today the data products of satelliteand lunar laser ranging are coordinated by the International Laser Ranging Service(ILRS) which was established in September 1998.For the historyandorganizationof the ILRS,refertoPearlman,Degnan,andBosworth(2Oo2).More information can be found at the ILRS website at URL[26].

# 5.4.3.1 Satellite Laser Ranging

Currentlythereareseveral satellitesavailableforlaserranging,butobservationsof the LAser GEOdynamics Satellites (LAGEOS)-1 and -2 currently provide the highest accuracy geodetic values.The Russian Etalon1and2 satelltes help to strengthen the determination ofEarth orientation parameters.LAGEOS-1 itself is a composite (aluminum shell and brass core）sphere, $6 0 \mathrm { c m }$ in diameter,withamass of $\approx 4 0 7 \mathrm { k g }$ ，with426 $3 . 8 \mathrm { c m }$ cube corner retroreflectors(CCRs) embedded initssurface.Itisinanearlycircularorbitataheight of about $5 9 0 0 \mathrm { k m }$ ,andan inclination of $1 0 9 . 8 ^ { \circ }$ .Itsrelatively large mass,small surface area,and high orbit result in low atmospheric drag and insensitivity to short-wavelength components of the Earth's gravity field.More information on LAGEOS and other such geodetic satellites isavailable in variousreferences listedatURL[27].

There are presently (2O12)36 SLR stations in regular operation,located on eight tectonic plates,regularly obtaining observational data by tracking approximately 3O satelites.The precision ofranges obtained from these instruments are on the order of a few milimeters (see Plag etal.2Oo9).These stations are operatedby various governmental anduniversity research organizations,in order to determine the satelite orbits and obtain various geophysical data relating to Earth orientation,station coordinates,changes in those values overtime (thereby giving tectonic-plate motion velocities),and long-wavelength components of the Earth's gravity field.Thecoordinates ofthe stations computedbythis technique have an originwhich isthought to be coincident with the center of mass of the Earth to withinafew millimeters. Earth-rotation results are computed at 1-day intervals (since 1993)using dataless thana week old, with polar motion accurate to O.130 milliarcseconds for recent times.As with the GNSS technique( $\ S 5 . 4 . 2 )$ ,UT1cannotbeindependentlydetermined fromSLRdata.However,SLR does determine,at one-day intervals,the time rateof change of UT1,or Length-Of-Day (LOD),with anaccuracy of approximately $4 0 \mu \mathrm { s }$ perday(Pavlis2O1O).Furtherinformation on the computational techniques involved is given in Kaula (1966,pp.78-81),and GSFC (1976,pp.7-11 to 7-15).

# 5.4.3.2Lunar Laser Ranging

Lunarlaser ranging (LLR) is identical in concept with SLR,except that the targets are arrays of cube corner reflectors placed on the Moon by the U.S.Apollo astronauts and USSR unmanned probes.However,the distance to the Moon makes it more difficult to point alaser reliably at the reflectors,and when there are return signals,they are orders of magnitude weaker than those from the LAGEOS satellites. Currently lunar laser ranging isperformed by the Centre d'Etudes et des Recherches Geodynamics et Astronomiques (CERGA) stationin Grasse (near Nice),France and the McDonald Observatory,Texas (MLRS2).Additionally,since September 2OO7 the Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser ranging Operation (APOLLO) in southern New Mexico delivers lunar laser ranging data, accurate at the $1 \mathrm { m m }$ level,on an operational basis (see Battat et al.2009).

Muchof the same information obtained with SLR canbeobtained fromLLR.However,in practice,with only a limited number of stations,the primary use of the LLR systemshasbeen for the determinationof UTO-UTC (and thereby UT1-UTC)，parameters ofthe Moon's orbit and the Moon’s rotation (librations)，special-and general-relativity parameter tests,and information on the tidal parameters of the Earth-Moon system.LLR also shows promise in providingquick determinations of UTO-UTC,since its computation isa simple matter as soon as the day's observations are completed at a given station. UTO-UTCiscurrentlydetermined irregularlywithanaccuracy of $\pm 0 . 3$ to $\pm 0 . 7 \mathrm { m s }$ (Mc-Carthy andLuzum,1991).With the improved ranging accuracy at several LLR stations down to $1 \mathrm { c m }$ ,thereisnow renewed interestinLLR applications (Kopeikin etal.2Oo8).For the history and science of lunar laser ranging,refer to Dickey etal.(1994) andWilliams and Dickey (2003).

# 5.4.4Doppler Orbitography and Radiopositioning Integrated by Satellite (DORIS)

The Doppler Orbitography and Radiopositioning Integrated by Satelite (DORIS） space geodetic techniquewas designed and developed by the Centre National d'Etudes Spatiales (CNES) for precise altimeter orbit determinations as well as geodetic ground-station positioning(Cretaux et al.1998;Soudarin and Crétaux 20O6).DORIS isan uplink system from ground stations to spacecraft that transmits at $2 0 3 6 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { M H z }$ and $4 0 1 . 2 5 ~ \mathrm { M H z }$ Asaspace geodetic technique,DORIS is unique in that it hasa balanced station distributionin the northern and southern hemispheres.There are more than 5O permanent beacons broadcasting to receiversonboardsevensatelites(SPOT-2,POT-4,POT-5,Jason-1,Jason-2,EIAT, andCryosat-2).Station positioning by the DORIS technique has a precisionof approximately $1 { - } 2 \mathrm { c m }$ .Dailypolar motiondeterminations have aprecision of approximately1-2mas.Like GNSS and laser ranging techniques,DORIS cannot determine UT1.

The International DORIS Service (IDS) was established on July1,20O3 to coordinate the acquisition and reduction of DORIS observations.The IDS website at URL[28] provides DORIS data products,including Earth orientation parameters,as well as more information about this technique.

# 5.4.5 Historical Methods

Various methods of lesser accuracy for EOP determination have been used for operational purposes in the 196Os through 198Os,with some stillin operational use today for special purposes.These techniques include primarily optical astrometric methods,such as astrolabes and photographic zenith tubes (PZTs),with space techniques such as Doppler satelite tracking and Connected-Element Radio Interferometry (CERI).

# 5.4.5.1Optical Astrometric Techniques

An explanation of the motion of the Earth's pole was first made by Chandler in the early 1890s (Chandler189la,1891b),using transit circle observations byKustner and others dating fromthe late 188Os.Chandler,and later others,also used various astrometric observations fromtransit circles,quadrants,and other visual optical instruments dating to the early 1700s. The189Os saw the establishment of the International Latitude Service (ILS),which operated several stations at approximately $3 9 ^ { \circ } \mathrm { N }$ latitude until the 197Os.The use of visual zenith tubes andthe invention of photographic zenith tubes (in 1913 by Ross) brought the measurement of polar motion to accuracy levels maintained into the 196Os.The use of precise pendulum clocks in the 193Os allowed the detection of seasonal variations in the length of day.Special Moon cameras were also used from 1952 until the early 196Os in order to measure changes in the length of day against Ephemeris Time (ET) derived from the observed position of the Moon.The same equipment was also used to compare ETwith atomic time and to establish the definition of the second (Markowitz et al.1958;Markowitz196O).The International Polar Motion Service (IPMS),following on the ILS,coordinated and reduced polar motion observations from a large number of observing sites.The Bureau International del'Heure (BIH)did the same forUTO-UTC related observations,and also eventually began to handle polar motion data in order to provide rapid service UT1-UTC.The optical techniques continued as the only source of EOP measurements until the mid-196Os,when Doppler satelite tracking began toprovideinformation on polar motion.More than 1Oo optical instruments all over the world were at one time in use for such measurements.The primary disadvantages (for improved EOP determination) of the optical astrometric techniques are their dependence on the local vertical as their primary reference and the accuracy of the star catalogs they use to define a celestial reference system.However,new solutions of EOP from optical astrometry by Vondrak etal.(1995)and Vondrak,Ron,and Stefva (2007) provide arich database for long-term Earth orientation study. The geodetic space-based techniques that have replaced the optical astrometric techniques are little affected by variations in the local vertical and provide instead for a crust-fixed,three-dimensional reference system. Information on these instruments and the various international services involved is given in reviews (Moritz and Mueller 1987,pp.324-337,424-430,456-465;Mueller1969,pp.80- 84;Lambeck 1980,pp.62-66;Munk and MacDonald,1960,pp.58-61;Bomford 1971, pp. 312,335-338.)

# 5.4.5.2Doppler SatelliteTracking

During the 196Os,Doppler tracking of the U.S.Navy Navigational Satellite System (NNSS) began to provide polar motion information on the same level of accuracy or better than the optical techniques.The polar motion data obtained via this system were used by the BIH in combination with the optically obtained values.However,this technique did suffer from the dependence of the EOP values on the particular satellites in use and from long-termdrifts,and as SLR became available for polar motion monitoring in the late 197Os,began to be phased outfor active EOPuse.In fact,GNSS (see $\ S 5 . 4 . 2 )$ is the successor of these Doppler systems. An excellent review of theNNSSDoppler satellite-tracking technique is given by Kouba (1983),and a review of EOP-related use of such tracking isgiven by Moritz and Mueller (1987,pp.338-344,467-470).

# 5.4.5.3 Connected-Element Radio Interferometry

Connected-Element Radio Interferometry (CERI) wasused to determine EOP in the late 1970sand 198Os.CERIoperates in the same fashionas VLBI (and,in fact,provided the initial tests of the VLBI principle),except that the radio telescopes are physically connected bya wideband communications link,so that the observations (delaysand delay rates—see $\ S 5 . 4 . 1 $ are made in real time.A least-squares solution to obtain EOP,station positions,and source positions can then be made as soon as observing ends for the day.Although the speed at which results could be made available was a strong point ofCERI,the small size of the arrays usually only allowed two EOP components to be determined simultaneously,such as $\Delta \phi$ and UTO-UTC.The U.S.Naval Observatory(USNO) operated such an interferometer at Green Bank,West Virginia,from October1978 until the end of 1987.Polar motionand UTO-UTC could be determined to an accuracy of about 5O mas and $4 . 6 \mathrm { m s }$ respectively (McCarthyet al.1985;Matsakis etal.1986).Another CERI operated fora short time (August-September, 1980)at Cambridge,UK(Klepczynski 1982). The eventual improvement in the turnaround timeand much higher accuracy of VLBI results replaced CERI for EOP determination.

# 5.4.6Alternative Techniques

Several observational methods are under development that show promise in the field of Earth orientation measurement.The use of laser-ring gyroscopes or superfluid helium interference gyroscopesmayallowchangesintheEarth'srotational velocityand $\Delta \phi$ to be monitored. These are of particular interest since they have no dependence on astronomical observations and may also be used as acheck on current techniques.Following are more detailed accounts of these two possible alternative observational systems.

# 5.4.6.1 Laser-Ring Gyroscopes

A laser light beam split and sent in opposite directions around a given path on the Earth's surface can be made to interfere with itself upon meeting again,producinga beat pattern. Changes in this interference pattern will occur because of changes in the Doppler shift of the light traversing the two different paths,due to variations in the Earth's rotation.Such adevice is knownasa laser-ring or Sagnac laser gyroscope (Soffel etal.1988;Stedman 1997).Theoretically,ring laser gyroscopes measure the absolute rotation of the Earth since onlyonemeasurementisrequired to gauge theEarth'sinstantaneousrotation.

The sensitivity of the ring laserdepends on the area enclosed.Ringlasers with an enclosed area between 1 and $8 3 3 ~ \mathrm { m } ^ { 2 }$ have been built. Sensor sensitivities range from $5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 0 }$ to $5 \times 1 0 ^ { - 1 2 } \mathrm { r a d / s } / \sqrt { \mathrm { H z } }$ Currently,the most stable ring lasers experience asignificant driftof $2 \times 1 0 ^ { - 6 }$ degrees per hour.Because of this stability,these sensors can detect the effect of daily and subdaily polar motion and tilt effects from solid Earth tides (Schreiber etal.2004). Currently,ring lasers have aresolution of a magnitude worse than techniques such as VLBI and GPS; it isanticipated that within the next decade there will be both an improvement in resolution and stability of the sensor(Schreiber et al. 2OO4;Plag etal.2009).

# 5.4.6.2 Superfluid Helium Interference Gyroscopes

The use of superfluid helium interference gyroscopes (SHIG) was proposed in 1988 for the determination of Earth rotation and polar motion(Packard1988).It involves the use of a small (lessthan 1-meter diameter) horizontal torusof superfluid helium( $^ 3 \mathrm { H e }$ or $^ 4 \mathrm { H e }$ ),inwhich ablockage with a pinhole is positioned.When this torus undergoes rotation (for example, because of the rotation of the Earth),it is possible to measure the phase difference or phase slips across the blockage (Schwab,Bruckner,and Packard1997).This isproportional to the rotational velocity ofthe torusand,therefore,the Earth,and could bemeasured to1part in $1 0 ^ { 9 }$ over afew seconds.Proof of concept experiments were conducted by Schwab,Bruckner, andPackard (1997) and Simmonds et al.(2Oo1),which clearly demonstrated that quantum interference in superfluid helium can detect small changes in the Earth'srotation.More recently,BrucknerandPackard(2OO3)and Sato,Joshi,andPackard (2OO8) demonstrated quantum mechanical phase coherence of superfuid $^ 4 \mathrm { H e }$ over a macroscopic length scale with long-term stability.It is feasible that superfluid helium interference gyroscopes will soon bea viable alternative for the determination of Earth orientation parameters independent of astronomical observations.

# 5.4.7 International Services

On1988 January1,the International Earth Rotation Service (IERS)(URL[29]) began operation,under theauspices of the IAU and the IUGG.It replaced the Bureau International de 'Heure(BIH)and the International PolarMotion Service (IPMS)as the organization responsible for determining and predicting the orientation of the Earth.The organization changed itsname on 2Oo3 January1to the International Earth Rotationand Reference SystemsService,but maintained the IERSacronym.The IERSchanged itsstructure on 2Oo1 January1. The following is a description of this new structure.TheIERS consists of a board of directors that overseesand controlsa“Central Bureau”,the manager of the IERS and executive arm of the“Directing Board”,and“Technique Centers”for each observational technique. An"Analysis Coordinator"ensures long-termand internal consistency ofIERS products and appropriate combination of technique centers’products into one set of official IERS products.“Product Centers”are responsible for the products of the IERS.“Special Bureaus”for the geophysical components are under the Global Geophysical Fluids Center.“Combination Centers”collectively combine inputs from different techniquesand develop new methods of datacombination.The“Directing Board’may establish“Working Groups”to investigate topics relevant to IERS components.

# 6.1 Introduction

Tospecify positions of celestial objects it is necessary to have a reference system.An ideal reference system would provide an inertial reference frame that could be used at all frequencies of the electromagnetic spectrum,and forall magnitudes or intensities.Anumber of problems,unfortunately,interfere with realizing such a reference system.Among thoseis thefact that most observers are situated on the Earth,an observing platform that is constantly changing in orientation with respect to inertial space.

This chapterdeals with those transformations necessary to determine the orientation of the Earth,the terrestrial reference system (TRS),with respect to the sky,the celestial reference system (CRS).It also describes the transformation from a reference system attached to a rotating Earth to that of a non-rotating object in the Earth's orbit about the solar system barycenter.This chapter does not include:those changes inthe reference systemarising from the Earth's nonlinear motion about the solar system barycenter,such as the annual aberration oflight;relativistic effects,such as the deflection of light by massive objects;or changes in coordinates arising from an observer being situated on the Earth's surface rather than at the geocenter,such as horizontal parallax or diurnal aberration of light.These topicsare discussed in Chapter7.Nor doesthis chapter deal with those changes in position caused bythe flexure of a non-rigid Earth,discussed in Chapter5.This chapter describes how The Astronomical Almanac determines the orientation of the TRS with respect to the CRS.The method of transforming from the CRS to the TRS underwent major changesin 2O03 and 2006.Both the old and new paradigmsare discussed.The procedures for these reductions arefound mainly in SectionBof The Astronomical Almanac and Chapter7of this book.

At the most basic level,the transformation for a vector, $\mathbf { X }$ ,from the TRS, ${ \bf x } _ { T R S }$ ,tothe CRS, $\mathbf { x } _ { C R S }$ ,iscarried outby the transformation:

$$
\mathbf { x } _ { C R S } = \mathbf { P } \mathbf { N } \mathbf { T } \mathbf { W } \mathbf { x } _ { T R S }
$$

where P, N, $\mathbf { T }$ and $\mathbf { W }$ are a set of rotation matrices that account for precession, nutation, Earth rotation,and polar motion,respectively.Each of these rotation matrices is the product of the three basic rotation matrices, ${ \bf R } _ { 1 } ( \theta ) , { \bf R } _ { 2 } ( \theta )$ ,and ${ \bf R } _ { 3 } ( \theta )$

$$
\begin{array} { r l } & { \mathbf { R } _ { 1 } ( \theta ) = \left[ \begin{array} { c c c } { 1 } & { 0 } & { 0 } \\ { 0 } & { \cos \theta } & { \sin \theta } \\ { 0 } & { - \sin \theta } & { \cos \theta } \end{array} \right] \qquad \mathbf { R } _ { 2 } ( \theta ) = \left[ \begin{array} { c c c } { \cos \theta } & { 0 } & { \sin \theta } \\ { 0 } & { 1 } & { 0 } \\ { - \sin \theta } & { 0 } & { \cos \theta } \end{array} \right] \quad \mathrm { ~ a n d ~ } } \\ & { \mathbf { R } _ { 3 } ( \theta ) = \left[ \begin{array} { c c c } { \cos \theta } & { \sin \theta } & { 0 } \\ { - \sin \theta } & { \cos \theta } & { 0 } \\ { 0 } & { 0 } & { 1 } \end{array} \right] } \end{array}
$$

where $\theta$ is the rotation angle,and the subscripts refer to rotations about the $x , y ,$ and $z$ axes, respectively,of a right-handed Cartesian reference system.Any rigid coordinate system can be mapped into any other rigid coordinate system with the same geometric origin bya series of three,non-unique,rotations (see Chapter14).

The first two matrices on the right hand side of Eq. 6.1, $\mathbf { P }$ andN,represent the dynamical motionof the Celestial Intermediate Pole (CIP; $\ S \ 6 . 2 . 3$ in response to the torques arising fromthe gravitational attraction of the Sun,Moon,and planets of the solar system.This motion has traditionally been separated into a secular part,precession,and a periodic part, nutation.Precession, $\mathbf { P }$ ,and nutation, $\mathbf { N }$ ,are treated as a separate transformations.The details of these transformations are covered in $\ S 6 . 6$ .

The third matrix on the right hand side of Eq.6.1, $\mathbf { T }$ ,is the rotation of the terrestrial reference systemwith respect to the celestial reference system.Thus,thismatrix is

$$
{ \bf T } = { \bf R } _ { 3 } ( T )
$$

where $T$ istheangle between the Terrestrial Intermediate Origin,TIO $( \ S 6 . 9 )$ ,and the origin of right ascension.In the past,the origin of right ascension was either the mean equinox of date,or the true equinox of date.Beginning in 20O3,the IAU (20old) recommends replacing the equinox and equator of datewith the Celestial Intermediate Origin,CIO $( \ S \ 6 . 7 )$ .The name for the angle $T$ depends on the origin of right ascension used.When the origin of rightascension is the mean equinox of date it iscalled theGreenwichMean Sidereal Time, GMST(g 6.8.5.1).When the origin of right ascension is the true equinox of date it is called the Greenwich Apparent Sidereal Time,GAST( $\textrm { \AA } 6 . 8 . 5 . 2 )$ .And when the origin of right ascension is the CIO it iscalled the Earth Rotation Angle,ERA( $\textrm { \AA } 6 . 8 . 4 )$ .The relationships between theERA,GMST,andGASTare discussed in $\ S 6 . 3$ and $\ S \ : 6 . 4$ .

Finally,the fourth matrix on the right hand sideofEq.6.1, $\mathbf { W }$ ,is the polar motion,and gives the position of the terrestrial reference frame with respect to the TIO ( $\lbrace 6 . 9 . 2 )$ .This matrix is

$$
{ \bf W } = { \bf R } _ { 1 } ( s ^ { \prime } ) { \bf R } _ { 2 } ( x ) { \bf R } _ { 1 } ( y )
$$

where $s ^ { \prime }$ is the TIO locator,and the angles $x$ and $y$ are the angular coordinates of the CIP $( \ S \ 6 . 2 . 3 )$ with respect to the terrestrial pole measured along the meridians at $0 ^ { \circ }$ and $2 7 0 ^ { \circ }$ $( 9 0 ^ { \circ } W )$ ,respectively.

# 6.2 The Celestial Reference System (CRS) and the Terrestrial Reference System (TRS)

Detailed information on the celestial reference system(CRS) itselfcan be found in Chapter4, and details of the terrestrial reference system(TRS) are in Chapter5.Both the CRS and the

TRS are rigid coordinate systems.Any such coordinate systemcan be described by a set of three mutually perpendicular coordinate axes.Alternatively,a direction vector, the pole;a plane perpendicular to that vector,the equator;anda fiducial point on the equator,the origin ofright ascension1,may describe the coordinate system.

Resolution A4 VII of theXXIst General Assembly of the International Astronomical Union (IAU 1992;URL[4]) defines the CRS.This resolution recommends the adoption of a celestial reference system based on VeryLong Baseline Interferometry (VLBI) observations of extragalactic radio sources aligned as near as possible to the mean equator of J2ooo.O with the origin of right ascension as near as possible to the dynamical equinox of J2ooo.0.This reference systemistobe,“...computed initially for the equator and equinox J2ooo.O(Julian date2451545.O TT)using the best available values of thecelestial pole offset with respect to the IAU expressions for precession and nutation...”A reference system based on this recommendation,the International Celestial Reference System (ICRS),was adopted in1997 inresolutionB2of theXXIIIrd General Assemblyof theIAU(IAU1997;URL[4]).The positive $z .$ -axis of the complimentary Cartesian coordinate is the north direction of the pole. The positive $x$ -axisis the direction towards the origin of right ascension.And thepositive $y$ -axis is the direction of $z \times x$ to give a right-handed coordinate system.

The ICRS is epochless,that is,unlike prior celestial reference systems it is not tied to the orientation of the Earth at some fiducial epoch.Instead,the only way in which the ICRS will change is if the positions of the defining sources on the sky are determined more accurately. Thus,although its initial construction aligned it with the best determination of the mean equatorand equinox of J2Ooo.0,subsequent improvement of the knowledge of that equator and equinox do not alter the orientation of the ICRS.Rather,the position of the mean equator and equinox in the ICRS isaltered.

It is necessary to distinguish whether the geometric origin is the solar system barycenter or theEarth's center of mass,the geocenter.If the origin is the solar system barycenter, itisreferred toas the BarycentricCelestial Reference System (BCRS);if the origin is the geocenter,it is referred toas the GeocentricCelestial ReferenceSystem(GCRS).TheGCRS and the BCRS are connected by a relativistic coordinate transformation (IAU 2Oo1a).In general,theGCRS shouldbe used when the objectofinterest is within severalEarth diameters of the geocenter.Beyond this distance,the BCRS should be used.It is common for users to refer to the ICRS when they specifically mean the BCRS;however,the term BCRS ought to be used in these cases to prevent confusion with the more general term.

Thegeometric origin of the Terrestrial Reference System(TRS) isthe geocenter.It is described by the three orthogonal reference directionswith respect to the body of the Earth. The Earth rotates.Thus,the TRS is a non-inertial reference system and its orientationin space is constantly changing.

Formany applications itis desirableto useaCelestial Intermediate Reference Systemthat is related to the TRSataparticular epoch.In the past the direction of the celestial ephemeris pole defined the equator of this system.The CIP replaced the celestial ephemeris pole after 1Jan.2003.

# 6.2.2The Conventional International Origin and the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF)

The principal axes of abody are the orthogonal directions that define a Cartesian coordinate systemin which the momentof inertia tensorisa diagonal matrix,i.e.,the products ofinertia are zero.For an oblate rigid elipsoid of revolution,two of the principal moments of inertia areequal,and the third principal moment is larger than the other two.In this case,the axis of thethird principal momentof inertiaisalso theaxisoffigure.TheEarth,however,is elastic and is not a perfect ellipsoid of revolution.Thus,its inertia tensor and the directions of the principal axesof inertiaare functions of time.Nevertheless,an instantaneous axis offigure, $F$ ,may be definedasthe line passing through the center of massof the Earth,parallel to the eigenvector of the instantaneous inertia tensor of the Earth.The intersection of $F$ with the surface of the Earth isthe instantaneouspoleoffigure.
