Inboth the planetocentric and planetographic systems,the fundamental reference $z$ -axisis the mean axis of rotation with the positive direction being toward the north pole.The equator is the plane passing through the center of mass of the planet,perpendicular to the $z$ -axis.The $x$ -axisisdefinedbythe intersectionof theplane of the equatorand the plane containing the prime meridian.The choice of the prime meridian is arbitrary.The $y$ -axisis then orthogonal to the other two axes and makes a right-handed coordinate system.The angular coordinates used inthese two systems are latitude and longitude.The range foraplanetocentric coordinate systemis the distance from the center of mass,while the range fora planetographic system istheheightabove orbelowareference surface.

Latitude (Fig.10.2) is measured north and south of the equator with the northern latitudes designated as positive.The planetocentric latitude of a point on the surface of a planet is the angle $\phi$ between the equatorial planeand the line segment connecting the point to the center ofmass.The planetographic latitude ofa pointistheangle $\phi ^ { \prime }$ between the line segment normal to the equipotential surface from that point to the equatorial plane.For a spherical object, the planetocentric and planetographic latitudes are identical.

Longitude is measured along the equator from the prime meridian.Planetocentric longitudes, $\lambda$ ,aremeasuredpositivelyin thecounterclockwisedirectionwhenviewed from the north pole.Planetographic longitudes, $\lambda ^ { \prime }$ ,on the other hand,are measured positively in the direction opposite to the object’s rotation.The planetographic longitude increases in the clockwise direction when viewed fromabove the north pole for an object with direct rotation. Planetocentric longitudesare measured from the ephemeris position of the prime meridian as defined by the rotational elements,while planetographic longitudes are measured from the cartographic position of the prime meridian as defined by the adopted longitude of some clearlyobservable surface feature.Forthe Earth,the Sun,and the Moon,longitudes are measured from $0 ^ { \circ }$ to $1 8 0 ^ { \circ }$ east and west.East longitude (counterclockwise when viewed from above the north pole) is commonly considered to be positive.

In the planetographic system,a point $P$ not on the reference surface is specified by the planetographic longitude and latitude of the point $P ^ { \prime }$ on the reference surface at which the normal line segment connecting $P$ to the equatorial plane passesthrough the reference surface and the height, $h$ ,of $P$ above orbelow the reference surface.

# 10.2.2 Coordinate Systems of Small Bodies

Aspherical or elipsoidal model shape has traditionally been used to define large bodies. For small,irregularlyshaped bodies,however,anellipsoid model isobviously inappropriate, except perhaps for dynamical studies.For very irregular bodies,the concept of a reference ellipsoid ceases to be useful for most purposes.For these bodies,topographic shapes are usually represented bya grid of radiito the surfaceasa function of planetocentric latitude and longitude.

Another difficulty for small bodiesis that two coordinates (i.e.,spherical angular measures)may not uniquely identify apointon the surface of the body.In other words it is possible to have a line fromthe center of the object intersect the surface more than once.This phenomenon can also happen on large and mostly elipsoidal objects such as the Earth,where features such as overhanging cliffs and natural bridges and arches may occur. Onlarge bodies such features are very small relative to the body sizeand often ignored at the scale of most topographic maps.For small bodies these featuresmay be fairly largerelative to the body size.However, even on small bodies this problem is usually restricted to small areas.But it still may make a planetocentric coordinate system difficult to use.Examples are on Eros (at a small patch west of Psyche),on Kleopatra (Ostro et al.2ooO),possibly on Toutatis near its ‘neck',and possibly near the south pole of Ida,where someradii may intersect the surface more than once.Cartographers have ad hoc tricks for a specific map,such as interpolating across the problemareafrom areas that are uniquely defined,or by showing overlapping contours.A Cartesian or other coordinate geometry may be preferable for arbitrarily complex shapes,such as a toroidal comet nucleus,where an active region has eaten its way through the nucleus.Such coordinate geometries may also be useful for irregular bodies imaged only on one side,such as19P/Borrelly and 81P/Wild 2.

Digital cartography has become increasingly popular with the introduction of large mass storage to computer systems. Cartographic databases are important when considering irregularly shaped bodiesand other bodies,where a filecontaining the coordinates for each pixel can describe the surface.In this case,the reference sphere has shrunk to apoint.If known, otherparameters such as brightness and gravitycan be associated with each pixel.With proper programming,pictorial and projected views of the body can then be displayed.

TheIAUWorkingGroup on Cartographic Coordinatesand Rotational Elements (Seidelmann et al.2oo2) recommends that longitudes on minor planets and comets should be measured,if possible,fromOto 36O degrees ina right-hand system fromadesignated prime meridian with the origin at the center of mass.For irregularbodies where orthographic digital projections are adopted to preserve the irregular appearance of the body without artificial distortion,these projections should follow the right-hand rule.

# 10.3 Phases and Magnitudes

# 10.3.1 Absolute and Apparent Magnitudes

Neglecting Variations from rotation or other intrinsic causes,the observed visual magnitude, V,for a solar system body is

$$
V = V ( 1 , 0 ) + 5 \log _ { 1 0 } ( d r ) + \Delta m ( i )
$$

where $d , r$ ,and $i$ ,showninFig.1O.3,are,respectively,theEarth-planet distanceinau,the planet's distance from the Sun inau,and the phase angle. $\Delta m ( i )$ is the correction for the variation in brilliancy of the planet with phase angle,i. $V ( 1 , 0 ) ^ { 3 }$ is the magnitude of the body at1au from the Sun,seen ata distance of1au,with a phase angle of $0 ^ { \circ }$ .

The quantity $\Delta m ( i )$ is determined empirically.It is the result of two effects.The first source of variation arises from the fraction of the illuminated disk visible from the Earth as it varieswith $i$ .The second effect is due to the properties of diffuse reflection from the object's surface and/or atmosphere.The parameter $\Delta m ( i )$ forplanetsand satellitesis expressed asa power series in $i$ ,retainingasmany termsas necessaryto represent theobservations.4For the outer planets a single term is usually sufficient,hence $\Delta m ( i )$ for the planets is usually referred to as the phase coefficient.

The tabulated magnitude of an object is the integrated total visual magnitude.The magnitudes forMercuryand Venus are from Hilton (2Oo5).Those of the other planets and Pluto5 are based on Harris(1961)with values for $V ( 1 , 0 )$ from de Vaucouleurs (1970) for all but Jupiter, whose $V ( 1 , 0 )$ value is from Irvine et al. (1968). The solar phase angle, $i$ ,is the apparentangular distance at the object between the Sunand the Earth.ForMercury,Venus,the Moon,and manyNearEarth Objects (NEOs),such as the Apollos,theAmors,andtheAtens, which cross the orbits of the Earth and/or Mars, $i$ may become very large or even go through the entire range of phase angle.For Marsand its satellites $i$ can be as large as $4 7 ^ { \circ }$ ,and for Jupiter and its satellites $i$ can be as large as $1 2 ^ { \circ }$ .Thus,for the main belt minor planets, $i$ may besubstantial.For the rest of the planets,satellites,and Kuiper belt objects the maximum value of $i$ is onlyafewdegrees.

Manyobjects showan opposition surge in their luminosities at small phase angles（ $5 ^ { \circ }$ orless).The exact cause of the opposition surge is not completely understood; however, the size of the surge depends on both the composition and size of the constituent particles of the body's regolith.The literature contains many studies (e.g.,Nelson et al.2ooo)attempting to both understand and quantify this phenomenon.

If the intrinsic properties of a planet cause its brightness to vary,the problem of predicting the apparent magnitude at a given time is considerably more complicated.The causes of intrinsic variability are the existence of surface markings on the planet,and changes in the planet's atmosphere,such asclouds,or volatile surface markings,such as theMartian ice caps.Surface markings on an object give rise to brightness variations that depend on the rotationof the object.Atmosphere changes or volatile surface markings give rise to variations of an irregular nature.Atmospheric variations may be short-lived,such as sand storms on Mars,or persist for long periods of time,such as the Great Red Spot on Jupiter.Because the atmospheric variationsand volatile markingsare erratic,their effects ona planet'salbedo cannot be predicted with accuracy,although some,such as the Martian ice caps,generally followa regular cycle.None of these erratic or atmospheric variations are currently taken into account in The Astronomical Almanac.

For some purposes,it is convenient to use the mean opposition magnitude, $V _ { 0 }$ ,which is related to $V ( 1 , 0 )$ by

$$
V _ { 0 } = V ( 1 , 0 ) + 5 \log _ { 1 0 } \left[ a ( a - 1 ) \right]
$$

where $a$ is the semimajor axis of the planet's orbit in au.

# 10.3.2 Surface Brightness

The surface brightness, $S B$ ,ofanobjectis the average brightness value for the illuminated portion of the apparent disk.The units for the surface brightness are visual magnitudes per square arcsecond.Assuming the body is anoblate spheroid,the apparent geometric area, $A$ , of the apparent disk is

$$
A = \pi a b ^ { \prime }
$$

where $^ a$ is the equatorial radius of the object, $b ^ { \prime }$ is the apparent polar radius of the object. Then,assuming the fractionof light forward scatteredby the atmosphere is insignificant, the surface brightness is

$$
S B = V + 2 . 5 \log _ { 1 0 } ( k A )
$$

where $k$ is the geometric fraction of the objectilluminated(Eq.10.33).Ifa significant amount of lightis forward scattered,as can happen with Venus near inferior conjunction(e.g.,Hilton 2005),the total area of the planet seen by the observer may be significantly greater than the apparent geometric area.

# 10.4 The Apparent Disk of a Solar System Body

Most bodies with diameters $\gtrsim 5 0 0 ~ \mathrm { k m }$ ,suchasaplanet,dwarf planet,or large satellite,are essentially oblate spheroids inshape with asemimajor axis, $a$ ,andasemiminor axis, $^ b$ The apparent disk of such abody is an ellipse with an apparent flattening, $f ^ { \prime }$ ,dependent on the apparent tilt of the object's axis.The apparent flattening of the object is always less than or equal to its true flattening. The apparent polar radius and the apparent flattening of an object are derived from the parametric equation for an ellipse:

\scriptstyle r = { \sqrt { x ^ { 2 } + y ^ { 2 } } }

The planetographic coordinates of the sub-Earth and sub-solar points are determined as follows:First,the position ephemerides are used to calculate the heliocentric equatorial rectangularcoordinates forthe body, $\mathbf { r }$ ,andtheEarth, $\mathbf { r } _ { e }$ at a given JulianDate in TT[JD(TT)]. $$

$$

The light-time correction, $\delta t$ , is found from $\Delta \mathbf { r }$ by dividing bythe speedoflight, $c$

$$

# 10.6.1.3 Calculating Topocentric Optical Librations

The differential corrections to be applied to the tabular geocentric librations to form the topocentric librationsare derived in Atkinson (1951).The formulae for the topocentric corrections are

$$
\begin{array} { r l } & { \Delta \lambda _ { m } = - H P ^ { \prime } \frac { \sin ( Q _ { m } - C _ { m } ) } { \cos b } } \\ & { \Delta \beta _ { m } = + H P ^ { \prime } \cos ( Q _ { m } - C _ { m } ) } \\ & { \Delta C _ { m } = + \sin ( \beta _ { m } - \Delta \beta _ { m } ) l - H P ^ { \prime } \sin Q \tan b } \end{array}
$$

where $\lambda _ { m }$ is the selenographic longitude, $\beta _ { m }$ is the selenographic latitude, $C _ { m }$ is the position angle of the axis of rotation, $Q _ { m }$ is the geocentric parallactic angle subtended by the Moon, and $H P ^ { \prime }$ istheparallax between the point of observation on the Earth's surface and the geocenter, commonly called the diurnal parallax.

The horizontal parallax, $H P$ ,occasionally called the geocentric horizontal parallax,is found from the Earth-Moon distance, $| \mathbf { r } _ { m } |$ ,and the equatorial radius of the Earth, $R _ { e } = { \ }$ 6378.137 km.

$$
\sin H P = { \frac { R _ { e } } { | \mathbf { r } _ { m } | } } .
$$

The geocentric (a.k.a.diurnal) parallax, $H P ^ { \prime }$ ,is computed from thehorizontal parallaxusing

$$
\sin H P ^ { \prime } = { \frac { \sin z \sin H P } { 1 - \sin z \sin H P } }
$$

where $Z$ is the geocentric zenith angle of the Moon. The values of $\boldsymbol { Q }$ and $z$ are calculated from the geocentric right ascension, $\alpha _ { m }$ ,and declination, $\delta _ { m }$ ,of theMoon:

$$
\begin{array} { c } { \sin z \sin Q = \cos \Phi \sin h } \\ { \sin z \cos Q = \cos \delta _ { m } \sin \Phi - \sin \delta _ { m } \cos \Phi \cos h } \\ { \cos z = \sin \delta _ { m } \sin \Phi + \cos \delta _ { m } \cos \Phi \cos h } \end{array}
$$

where $\Phi$ is the geocentric latitude of the observer and $\mathbf { h }$ is the local hour angle of the Moon,

$$
h = \mathrm { L S T } - \alpha _ { m }
$$

whereLST isthe apparent local sidereal time.

In the interpolation of the tabular geocentric librations to the time of observation,second differences must be taken into account.The semidiameter, $S D$ ,of theMoon,in radians,is

$$
S D = \tan ^ { - 1 } { \frac { R _ { m } } { | \mathbf { r } _ { m } | } }
$$

where $R _ { m } = 1 7 3 7 . 4 \mathrm { k m }$ is the radius of the Moon and $| \mathbf { r } _ { m } |$ is the Earth-Moon distance.

# 10.7Physical Ephemerides of the Planets

In addition to the quantities described in $\ S 1 0 . 3$ and10.4,the planetocentric orbital longitude of the Sun, $L _ { S }$ ,istabulatedforMars,Jupiter,Saturn,Uranus,andNeptuneinTheAstronomicalAlmanac,and forallof the planetsandPlutoinMICA.Thisangle ismeasured eastward

inthe body’sorbital plane from its instantaneous vernalequinox.The planet’s vernal equinox is the point where the ascending node of the planet's equator crosses the plane of its orbit about the Sun. Thus,

$$

$$
\mathbf { r } _ { p } \times \mathbf { r } _ { v e } .
$$

Values for $L _ { S }$ of $0 ^ { \circ }$ ， $9 0 ^ { \circ }$ ， $1 8 0 ^ { \circ }$ ,and $2 7 0 ^ { \circ }$ correspond to the beginning of spring,summer, autumn,and winter,respectively,in the body's northern hemisphere.

# 10.7.4 Jupiter

Multiple longitude systemsare defined forJupiter.Each systemcorrespondstoa different apparent rate of motion.System II, which applies to the origin of radio emissions on Jupiter, is the preferred system.System Iapplies to the mean atmospheric equatorial rotation of the planet.System II applies to the mean atmospheric rotation north of the south component of the north equatorial belt and south of the north component of the south equatorial belt of Jupiter. The rotation rates for both System Iand System II are uncertain and subject to change.The sub-Earth and sub-solarlongitudes tabulated in The Astronomical Almanac are based on System III.

Jupiter is the most luminous of the planets.The value used for $V ( 1 , 0 )$ istaken from Irvine et al.(1968).Variations in its brilliancy with rotation are small, $\sim 0 . 0 1$ mag. and irregular. # 10.7.5 Saturn

For Saturn only System II [observed rotation of radio emissions;Desch and Kaiser(1981)] rotations are defined.Galopeau and Lecacheux (2Ooo) noted that the apparent period of Saturn’sradio emissions vary on the order of a percent over periods of years.The angular momentum of the planet is orders of magnitude too large to allow for such large changes in the rotation period over such a short period of time.Gurnett et al.(2OO7) conclude that there is a slippage between the System II rotation rate arising in Saturn's plasma disk,and that its internal rotation is the source forthis discrepancy.Thus,the useof Saturn'sradio emissions as an indicator for its rotation rate isin question.

Like Jupiter,the mean rotation rate of the atmosphere is uncertain.The previously defined System Irotation system has been found to be of litte use since the Voyager encounters with Saturn,and is no longer supported by the IAU.

Because of the high brilliance of Saturn's rings,the brilliance of the planet alone is not well known.Thus,the combined planet-ring magnitude and surface brightness are tabulated.
